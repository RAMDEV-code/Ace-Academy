Biology is the scientific study of living things (organisms). It explores their structures, functions, growth, origin, evolution, and relationships with the environment. The word “Biology” comes from the Greek words bios (life) and logos (study).
Branches of biology include botany (study of plants), zoology (study of animals), and microbiology (study of microorganisms). Other branches like ecology, genetics, physiology, and anatomy focus on specific aspects of life.
Biology uses scientific methods—observation, questioning, hypothesis, experimentation, analysis, and conclusion—to investigate life processes. It connects to other sciences like chemistry and physics.
Biology is important in medicine, agriculture, environmental conservation, biotechnology, and everyday health decisions.

Living things share basic characteristics: movement, respiration, nutrition, excretion, growth, reproduction, sensitivity, and cellular structure. These are remembered with the acronym MR NIGER C.
Plants and animals differ in nutrition—plants are autotrophic (make their own food through photosynthesis), while animals are heterotrophic (depend on other organisms for food).
Plants usually have rigid cell walls made of cellulose, chloroplasts for photosynthesis, and store food as starch. Animals lack cell walls, do not have chloroplasts, and store food mainly as glycogen.
Respiration, movement, and reproduction occur in both, but the methods differ. For example, plants exhibit slow growth movement, while animals often move rapidly.

Classification is the arrangement of organisms into groups based on similarities and differences. It helps in identification, naming, and studying organisms efficiently.
Early classification was artificial, using observable features, but modern classification is natural, based on evolutionary relationships.
Carolus Linnaeus introduced the binomial nomenclature system, giving every organism a two-part Latin name: the genus (capitalized) and the species (lowercase), e.g., Homo sapiens.
The major taxonomic ranks are Kingdom, Phylum/Division, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species.

Living things are grouped into five kingdoms:
Monera – prokaryotic, unicellular organisms like bacteria.


Protista – eukaryotic, mostly unicellular organisms like amoeba.


Fungi – multicellular (except yeast), heterotrophic, cell walls of chitin.


Plantae – multicellular, autotrophic, cellulose cell walls.


Animalia – multicellular, heterotrophic, no cell walls.


Each kingdom has unique structural and functional features. For example, fungi absorb nutrients from decaying matter, while protists may be autotrophic or heterotrophic.
Modern classification may also include domains—Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya—based on genetic and biochemical differences.

Life is organized into levels, from smallest to largest: cell → tissue → organ → system → organism.
The cell is the basic unit of life, performing all vital functions. In multicellular organisms, similar cells group into tissues (e.g., muscle tissue). Tissues form organs (e.g., heart), which work together in systems (e.g., circulatory system) to perform complex functions.
In plants, examples include epidermal tissue (protection), xylem and phloem (transport), and leaves (organs) forming part of the shoot system.
Specialization allows efficiency—different cells are adapted to different functions, leading to interdependence within the organism.

Nutrient cycling is the movement and exchange of essential elements like carbon, nitrogen, and water between living organisms and the environment. These cycles ensure that nutrients are reused and not wasted.
The carbon cycle involves processes such as photosynthesis (plants absorb carbon dioxide to make food), respiration (organisms release CO₂), decomposition (dead matter breaks down), and combustion (burning fuels releases CO₂).
The nitrogen cycle includes nitrogen fixation (bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen to nitrates), nitrification (conversion of ammonium to nitrates), assimilation (plants absorb nitrates), ammonification (decomposers release ammonia), and denitrification (bacteria return nitrogen to the atmosphere).
The water cycle involves evaporation, condensation, precipitation, infiltration, and transpiration. It regulates climate and provides water for all living things.
Human activities like deforestation, pollution, and burning fossil fuels disrupt these cycles, leading to climate change, eutrophication, and loss of biodiversity.

Nutrition in animals is the process of obtaining and utilizing food substances for growth, repair, and energy. It is classified into autotrophic (self-feeding, e.g., plants) and heterotrophic (dependent feeding, e.g., animals).
Animals exhibit heterotrophic nutrition, which can be holozoic, parasitic, or saprophytic. Holozoic nutrition involves ingestion, digestion, absorption, assimilation, and egestion.
Digestion may be intracellular (inside cells, e.g., Amoeba) or extracellular (in a digestive tract, e.g., humans). In humans, digestion is aided by enzymes like amylase, pepsin, and lipase.
The human digestive system includes the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, rectum, and anus. Accessory organs include the liver, pancreas, and salivary glands.
Balanced diet, water, vitamins, and minerals are vital for healthy functioning, while poor nutrition can lead to malnutrition and related diseases.

Mode of nutrition refers to the way organisms obtain food and energy. It is broadly classified into autotrophic and heterotrophic modes.
Autotrophic nutrition includes photosynthesis (plants making food using light, CO₂, and water) and chemosynthesis (bacteria using chemical reactions to make food).
Heterotrophic nutrition includes:
Holozoic – ingestion and digestion of solid food (e.g., humans).


Parasitic – living on or in another organism and deriving food at its expense (e.g., tapeworm).


Saprophytic – feeding on dead and decaying matter (e.g., fungi).


Symbiotic – mutual benefit between two organisms (e.g., lichen).


Mode of nutrition determines the organism’s role in the food chain and ecosystem stability.

Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their environment. The environment consists of biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) factors.
An ecosystem is a community of organisms interacting with their physical environment. Examples include forests, deserts, ponds, and grasslands.
Ecological hierarchy includes organism → population → community → ecosystem → biome → biosphere.
Habitat is where an organism lives; niche is its functional role. Abiotic factors like temperature, light, water, and soil affect distribution and abundance of organisms.
Energy flows in ecosystems through food chains, food webs, and trophic levels, while matter is recycled through nutrient cycles.

Growth is the permanent increase in size, mass, and number of cells in an organism. It results from cell division (mitosis), cell enlargement, and cell differentiation.
In plants, growth is indefinite and occurs mainly at meristems (root and shoot tips). In animals, growth is definite and occurs throughout the body.
Growth can be measured using parameters such as height, weight, volume, or dry mass. Growth curves often show lag phase, log phase, and stationary phase.
Factors affecting growth include genetic makeup, nutrition, hormones, and environmental conditions like temperature, water, and light.
In humans, growth is influenced by growth hormone, while in plants, it is influenced by auxins, gibberellins, and cytokinins.

Ecology is the branch of biology that studies how living organisms interact with one another and with their environment. The environment consists of both abiotic factors (non-living elements like temperature, water, soil, light, and minerals) and biotic factors (living organisms such as plants, animals, and microorganisms).
An ecosystem is the functional unit of ecology, consisting of all living organisms in an area and their interactions with the physical environment. Ecosystems can be terrestrial (land-based) or aquatic (water-based). Within ecosystems, energy flows from producers to consumers and decomposers, while nutrients are recycled.
The biosphere is the global sum of all ecosystems, where life exists. Population refers to all individuals of the same species living in a given area, while a community is the sum of all populations in that area. Ecology also examines habitats (where an organism lives) and niches (the role an organism plays in its environment).
Understanding ecological concepts is vital for conserving biodiversity and maintaining balance in nature.

Ecological relationships describe the interactions between organisms in a community. These interactions can be beneficial, harmful, or neutral depending on the species involved. Three important relationships are mutualism, parasitism, and commensalism.
Mutualism is a relationship where both species benefit. Examples include flowers and pollinating insects (insects get nectar, plants get pollinated) and nitrogen-fixing bacteria in legume roots (bacteria get shelter, plants get nitrogen). Mutualism can be obligatory (partners cannot survive without each other) or facultative (beneficial but not essential).
Parasitism is a relationship where one organism (the parasite) benefits while the other (the host) is harmed. Parasites can be endoparasites (inside the host, e.g., tapeworms) or ectoparasites (on the host's surface, e.g., lice). Parasitism often leads to disease or reduced fitness in the host.
Commensalism is a relationship where one species benefits while the other is neither helped nor harmed. Examples include barnacles attaching to whales for transport and remora fish riding on sharks for scraps.
These relationships play a crucial role in shaping ecosystems, influencing species distribution, and maintaining ecological balance.

Tolerance in ecology refers to the range of environmental conditions an organism can survive and reproduce in. These conditions include temperature, salinity, light intensity, pH, and moisture. Every species has a tolerance range, which includes the optimum range (most suitable conditions), and limits of tolerance (extreme conditions beyond which survival is impossible).
The law of tolerance states that the existence, abundance, and distribution of organisms depend on the availability of environmental factors within their tolerance range. Organisms with a wide tolerance range (eurytopic species) can survive in many conditions, while those with a narrow range (stenotopic species) are more restricted.
Tolerance is influenced by genetics, adaptation, and previous exposure to stress. Understanding tolerance helps predict species distribution and ecosystem changes.

Adaptation refers to the structural, physiological, and behavioral features that enable organisms to survive and reproduce in their environment. Adaptations can be classified into structural adaptations (physical features), physiological adaptations (internal functions), and behavioral adaptations (habits or actions).
Structural adaptations include features like thick fur in polar bears, long roots in desert plants, and streamlined bodies in fish. Physiological adaptations include a camel’s ability to store water, or the production of antifreeze proteins in polar fish. Behavioral adaptations include migration, hibernation, and nocturnal activity.
Adaptations arise over many generations through the process of natural selection. Organisms that are better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and pass on their traits.

Adaptations can be specific to different environments such as terrestrial, aquatic, aerial, desert, and extreme habitats. Human activities can influence adaptation rates, either by creating new challenges (pollution, habitat loss) or by aiding survival (breeding programs). Adaptations are vital for species survival in changing environments.

Pollution is the introduction of harmful substances or energy into the environment, causing damage to living organisms and ecosystems. It can occur naturally, but most pollution is caused by human activities such as industrialization, farming, and transportation.
Major types include air pollution (smoke, carbon monoxide, greenhouse gases), water pollution (oil spills, sewage, chemicals), land pollution (solid waste, plastics, pesticides), and noise pollution (loud machinery, traffic). Each type affects health and environmental balance differently.
Air pollution causes respiratory diseases and global warming; water pollution leads to death of aquatic life; land pollution reduces soil fertility; noise pollution causes stress and hearing loss.
Control measures include proper waste disposal, recycling, use of clean energy sources, reforestation, and enforcing environmental laws. Education and public awareness are essential for preventing and reducing pollution.

 Natural resources are materials or substances occurring in nature, such as air, water, soil, forests, minerals, and wildlife. They are classified into renewable (can be replaced naturally, e.g., forests, fish, water) and non-renewable (finite, e.g., petroleum, coal, minerals).
Conservation means the sustainable management and use of natural resources to prevent depletion and ensure availability for future generations. Over-exploitation, pollution, and habitat destruction threaten these resources.
Methods of conservation include afforestation, reforestation, regulated harvesting, recycling, wildlife protection laws, use of alternative energy sources, and public education. For non-renewable resources, efficient usage and substitution are essential.
Organizations such as the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and local environmental agencies play a key role in global and national conservation efforts. Community participation is equally important.

Reproduction is the biological process by which living organisms produce new individuals of the same species, ensuring survival and continuity of life. It is a fundamental characteristic of living things.
There are two main types: asexual reproduction (one parent, genetically identical offspring, e.g., binary fission, budding) and sexual reproduction (two parents, genetic variation, e.g., fertilization in animals and plants).
Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of male and female gametes, resulting in offspring with traits from both parents. This promotes genetic diversity, which is essential for adaptation and survival in changing environments.
Asexual reproduction is faster and less energy-demanding but lacks genetic variation. Reproduction is influenced by environmental factors, health of the organism, and availability of resources.

Smaller organisms, such as bacteria, protozoa, fungi, and some plants, often reproduce asexually due to their simple structures and rapid life cycles.
Common methods include binary fission (equal division into two cells, e.g., bacteria), budding (small outgrowth forms and develops, e.g., yeast), spore formation (resistant reproductive cells, e.g., fungi), and fragmentation (body breaks into parts that grow into new organisms, e.g., some algae).
These methods are efficient, require less energy, and allow quick population increase. However, they produce genetically identical offspring, making populations vulnerable to diseases and environmental changes.
Some smaller organisms, such as protozoa, can also reproduce sexually under certain conditions, introducing variation and improving adaptability.

Micro-organisms are tiny living organisms that can only be seen under a microscope. They include bacteria, fungi, protozoa, algae, and viruses. They exist everywhere — in air, water, soil, food, and even inside living organisms.
They are classified based on their cell structure and mode of nutrition. Bacteria are single-celled prokaryotes, fungi can be unicellular or multicellular, protozoa are animal-like protists, and algae are plant-like protists. Viruses are non-cellular and require a host to reproduce.
Micro-organisms thrive in different environments such as hot springs, ice, oceans, animal intestines, and decaying matter. Some require oxygen (aerobic), while others survive without oxygen (anaerobic).
They play important roles in nature such as decomposing dead organisms, forming part of food chains, and helping in nutrient recycling. However, some can cause diseases in humans, animals, and plants.
Micro-organisms spread through air, water, direct contact, food, and vectors like insects. Preventing harmful micro-organisms involves proper hygiene, clean water supply, and food safety measures.

Culturing micro-organisms means growing them under controlled laboratory conditions. The main purpose is to study their characteristics, identify species, and produce useful products.
Culturing requires a suitable medium, which may be liquid (broth) or solid (agar). Media contain nutrients like carbon, nitrogen, minerals, and sometimes growth factors. Common types include nutrient agar, potato dextrose agar, and blood agar.
Micro-organisms need suitable conditions for growth such as temperature, pH, moisture, and oxygen level. For example, mesophiles grow best at moderate temperatures, while thermophiles thrive in heat.
Aseptic techniques are essential to prevent contamination, such as sterilizing equipment, flaming tools, and working near a Bunsen burner. Inoculation introduces the micro-organism into the medium, followed by incubation at an optimal temperature.
Cultures can be pure (one species) or mixed (more than one species). Pure cultures are important for accurate study and industrial applications. Identification is done using colony morphology, staining, and biochemical tests.

Micro-organisms actively participate in many biological and industrial processes. In nature, they decompose organic matter, recycling nutrients like nitrogen, carbon, and sulfur.
In food production, bacteria and fungi are used in making bread, cheese, yogurt, beer, and wine. In agriculture, nitrogen-fixing bacteria enrich soil fertility, while others help in composting.
In medicine, micro-organisms produce antibiotics (e.g., Penicillium for penicillin), vaccines, and vitamins. They are also used in biotechnology for genetic engineering, enzyme production, and fermentation.
Micro-organisms help in sewage treatment by breaking down organic waste and purifying water. Some degrade oil spills and other pollutants in bioremediation.
However, harmful micro-organisms cause spoilage, contamination, and disease outbreaks. Their “action” can be beneficial or harmful depending on the context. Understanding their behavior allows scientists to harness the good and control the bad.

STIs are infections spread mainly through sexual contact with an infected person. They can be caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or protozoa.
Common bacterial STIs include gonorrhea, syphilis, and chlamydia. Viral STIs include HIV/AIDS, herpes, and human papillomavirus (HPV). Protozoan STIs include trichomoniasis, while fungal STIs include candidiasis.
Symptoms vary but may include genital sores, discharge, itching, pain during urination, and swollen lymph nodes. Some STIs, like HIV, weaken the immune system and can be life-threatening.
STIs spread through unprotected sex, sharing needles, blood transfusions, and from mother to child during childbirth.
Prevention includes abstinence, consistent condom use, regular testing, faithful relationships, and prompt treatment. Education and awareness campaigns reduce stigma and promote early diagnosis.

 Better health refers to the state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not just the absence of disease. Good health is maintained through personal hygiene, balanced diet, regular exercise, and adequate rest. Public health focuses on preventing diseases through immunization, proper sanitation, and safe water supply. Diseases can be communicable (e.g., cholera, measles) or non-communicable (e.g., diabetes, hypertension). Communicable diseases spread through agents such as water, air, food, insects, and direct contact. Prevention methods include vaccination, use of insecticide-treated nets, handwashing, and proper waste disposal. Government agencies like the Ministry of Health and WHO help in health campaigns, disease control, and provision of medical facilities. Health education also plays a key role in promoting healthy lifestyles and reducing risk factors.

Population refers to the total number of individuals of a species in a given area at a given time. Population studies help ecologists understand species distribution, abundance, and density.
 A quadrat is a square or rectangular frame used to define a sample area for counting organisms, usually plants or slow-moving animals. To estimate population size, several quadrats are placed randomly in the study area. The average number of organisms per quadrat is calculated, then multiplied by the total area.
 Population size can be influenced by birth rate, death rate, immigration, and emigration. Environmental factors such as temperature, rainfall, soil quality, and human activities also affect populations.
 Accurate population data is essential for conservation, agriculture, and resource management.
 Quadrat sampling is more effective for immobile organisms and requires random placement to avoid bias.

An ecosystem is the interaction between living organisms (biotic components) and their non-living environment (abiotic components) in a specific area. Examples include forests, ponds, and grasslands.
 Biotic components include producers (plants), consumers (animals), and decomposers (fungi, bacteria). Abiotic components include sunlight, air, water, soil, and temperature.
 Ecosystem functioning involves energy flow, nutrient cycling, and population interactions (competition, predation, parasitism, mutualism). Energy flows in one direction from the sun → producers → consumers → decomposers. Nutrients are recycled within the ecosystem through decomposition.
 The balance of an ecosystem can be disrupted by human activities such as deforestation, pollution, and overhunting.
 Maintaining a stable ecosystem is essential for biodiversity conservation and sustainability.

Energy transformation in nature refers to the conversion of energy from one form to another to sustain life processes. The primary source of energy for most life forms is the sun.
 Through photosynthesis, producers (green plants) convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. Herbivores feed on plants to obtain this energy, carnivores feed on herbivores, and decomposers break down dead organisms, releasing nutrients.
 Energy flows through food chains and food webs, with only about 10% transferred to the next trophic level; the rest is lost as heat.
 Other energy transformations include respiration (chemical energy to kinetic and heat energy), fermentation, and chemosynthesis in some bacteria.
 Understanding energy transformation helps in managing natural resources and conserving energy in human systems.
