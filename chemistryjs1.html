        Chemistry is the branch of science that studies the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter.
Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space.
Chemistry explains how substances are formed, their uses, and how they react with each other.
It is divided into branches such as organic chemistry, inorganic chemistry, physical chemistry, analytical chemistry, and biochemistry.
Chemistry is important in medicine, agriculture, food production, industry, and environmental protection.
The study of chemistry involves laboratory work, experiments, and observation.
Laboratory safety rules must be followed to prevent accidents.
Some basic laboratory equipment includes test tubes, beakers, measuring cylinders, Bunsen burners, and thermometers.
Common careers in chemistry include pharmacists, chemical engineers, laboratory scientists, and forensic scientists.
Understanding chemistry helps us solve everyday problems and improve life.

        Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space.


It exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas.


Solids have definite shape and volume.


Liquids have definite volume but no definite shape.


Gases have neither definite shape nor volume.


Matter is made up of tiny particles called atoms or molecules.


These particles are always in motion.


The arrangement of particles differs in solids, liquids, and gases.


Matter can undergo physical or chemical changes.


Examples of matter include water, air, stone, and iron.

        Change of state is the transformation of matter from one form to another.


It occurs when heat is added or removed.


Melting is the change from solid to liquid.


Freezing is the change from liquid to solid.


Boiling or evaporation is the change from liquid to gas.


Condensation is the change from gas to liquid.


Sublimation is the change from solid to gas without becoming liquid.


Deposition is gas changing directly to solid.


The change of state is a physical change.


Water is a common substance used to observe change of state.

        Elements are classified as metals and non-metals.


Metals are usually hard, shiny, and good conductors of heat and electricity.

Non-metals are brittle and cannot be stretched into wires.


Examples of metals include iron, copper, and gold.


Examples of non-metals include oxygen, carbon, and sulfur.


Metals are usually found in the left side of the periodic table.


Non-metals are found on the right side of the periodic table.


Both metals and non-metals are important in everyday life.

        Elements are pure substances made up of only one type of atom.


The chemical symbol of an element is usually one or two letters.


The first letter of a symbol is always capitalized; the second (if any) is lowercase.


There are over 100 elements, but the first 30 are essential in basic chemistry.


Examples: Hydrogen (H), Helium (He), Lithium (Li), Beryllium (Be).


These elements are listed in order of their atomic number.


Atomic number is the number of protons in an atom’s nucleus.


Symbols help in writing chemical formulas and equations.


Memorizing the first 30 elements builds a foundation for chemistry.


Some elements have Latin-based symbols like Sodium (Na) and Potassium (K).

        The periodic table arranges elements based on atomic number.


Rows in the periodic table are called periods.


Columns are called groups or families.


Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties.


The table is divided into metals, non-metals, and metalloids.


Group 1 elements are called alkali metals; they are very reactive.


Group 18 elements are noble gases; they are very stable.


The periodic table helps predict the behavior of elements.


Dmitri Mendeleev created the first version of the periodic table.


Modern periodic table is arranged by increasing atomic number.

        A compound is a substance made of two or more different elements chemically combined.


Compounds have properties different from the elements that form them.


They are represented by chemical formulas (e.g., H₂O for water).


Compounds form through chemical bonds like ionic and covalent bonds.


Water is a compound made from hydrogen and oxygen.


Table salt (NaCl) is a compound of sodium and chlorine.


Compounds can be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.


They always have a fixed ratio of atoms.


Compounds are different from mixtures, which are not chemically combined.


Most substances around us are compounds.

        A mixture is made by combining two or more substances physically, not chemically.


The substances in a mixture retain their individual properties.


Mixtures can be homogeneous or heterogeneous.


In a homogeneous mixture, the composition is uniform (e.g., salt water).


In a heterogeneous mixture, the composition is not uniform (e.g., sand and water).


Mixtures can be separated by physical methods like filtration, evaporation, or distillation.


Air is a mixture of gases.


Mixtures do not have a fixed composition.


Unlike compounds, no chemical reaction occurs when forming mixtures.


Examples: salad, salt water, muddy water.

        A mixture is a combination of two or more substances not chemically combined.


Sieving is a method used to separate mixtures containing solids of different sizes.


A sieve is a device with holes that allow smaller particles to pass through while retaining larger ones.


Common examples include separating sand from gravel or rice from stones.


Magnetization is used to separate magnetic materials from non-magnetic ones.


It works based on the magnetic properties of materials like iron, cobalt, or nickel.


A magnet is passed over the mixture, attracting only magnetic substances.


It is commonly used in recycling industries and mineral extraction.


Both methods are physical means of separation and do not change the substances.


They help improve purity and make substances easier to use or process.

        Filtration is a method used to separate insoluble solids from liquids.


It involves the use of filter paper or other porous materials.


The mixture is poured into a funnel lined with filter paper.


The liquid part, called the filtrate, passes through.


The solid part, called the residue, remains on the paper.


It is useful in separating sand from water or tea leaves from tea.


It is a physical method and does not alter the components.


Filtration is widely used in homes and industries.


It helps in purification and waste treatment.


Only mixtures with undissolved solids and liquids can be filtered.

        Atoms are the smallest particles of matter that retain the properties of an element.


Early Greek philosophers like Democritus proposed the idea of atoms as indivisible.


John Dalton's atomic theory (1803) described atoms as solid, indivisible spheres.


J.J. Thomson (1897) discovered the electron and proposed the "plum pudding" model.


Ernest Rutherford (1911) conducted the gold foil experiment, revealing a dense nucleus.


Rutherford’s model had electrons orbiting around a central nucleus.


Niels Bohr (1913) suggested electrons move in fixed energy levels (shells).


The quantum mechanical model describes the electron's probable location in orbitals.


Each new model corrected and improved the older one with new experimental evidence.


Atomic models help explain chemical properties and behavior of elements.

        An atom consists of three subatomic particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons.


Protons carry a positive charge and are located in the nucleus.


Neutrons have no charge and are also found in the nucleus.


Electrons are negatively charged and orbit the nucleus in energy levels.


The number of protons equals the atomic number of the atom.


Electrons usually equal the number of protons in a neutral atom.


The mass number equals the sum of protons and neutrons.


Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.


Electrons are arranged in shells: 2 in the first, 8 in the second, and so on.


The structure of an atom determines its chemical properties and reactivity.

        The kinetic theory explains the behavior of matter based on particle motion.


All matter is made of tiny particles in constant motion.


In solids, particles are tightly packed and vibrate in place.


In liquids, particles move freely but stay close together.


In gases, particles move rapidly and are widely spaced.


The temperature of a substance is related to the average kinetic energy of its particles.


Heating a substance increases the motion of its particles.


Cooling reduces particle movement and kinetic energy.


Pressure in gases is caused by particles hitting the container walls.


Kinetic theory explains changes in state and expansion/contraction of matter.

        Changes of state occur when matter absorbs or releases energy.


Melting is the change from solid to liquid due to heat.


Freezing is the change from liquid to solid when energy is lost.


Boiling or evaporation is the change from liquid to gas.


Condensation is the change from gas to liquid when cooled.


Sublimation is the direct change from solid to gas (e.g., dry ice).


All changes of state are physical changes; they do not change the substance's identity.


Energy affects particle motion and spacing during state changes.


At the melting and boiling points, temperature remains constant during the change.


Kinetic theory helps explain why these changes happen at certain temperatures.

        Acids are substances that taste sour and turn blue litmus paper red.


Bases are substances that feel slippery and turn red litmus paper blue.


Acids have a pH less than 7; bases have a pH greater than 7.


Common acids include hydrochloric acid (HCl), sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), and citric acid.


Common bases include sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and ammonia (NH₃).


Acids donate hydrogen ions (H⁺), while bases accept H⁺ or donate hydroxide ions (OH⁻).


Strong acids/bases fully ionize in water; weak ones only partially.


Bases can be classified as alkalis if they are soluble in water.


Acids and bases are used in food, cleaning, and industry.


The strength of an acid or base can be measured using indicators or pH meters.

        Litmus paper is a simple indicator: acids turn blue litmus red, bases turn red litmus blue.


Methyl orange turns red in acid and yellow in base.


Phenolphthalein is colorless in acids and pink in bases.


Universal indicator shows a range of colors depending on pH.


Natural indicators include red cabbage juice, turmeric, and hibiscus.


Test tubes, droppers, and beakers are used in these tests.


A few drops of the indicator are added to the test solution.


The observed color change reveals the pH level or nature of the solution.


Use distilled water for neutral controls.


Always wear goggles and gloves when handling acids and bases.

        A neutralization reaction occurs when an acid reacts with a base.


The products of neutralization are salt and water.


The reaction can be represented as: Acid + Base → Salt + Water.


It is an exothermic reaction (releases heat).


An example is HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O.


Neutralization is used in treating indigestion and acidic soil.


pH of the final solution is close to 7 (neutral).


Strong acid + strong base = neutral salt.


Weak acid + strong base = basic salt; strong acid + weak base = acidic salt.


Indicators help determine the end point of neutralization.
