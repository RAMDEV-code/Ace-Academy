        Biology is the scientific study of living organisms and their interactions with the environment.


The word “Biology” is derived from Greek words: bios (life) and logos (study).


Biologists study the structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, and distribution of living things.


Biology is divided into major branches such as Botany (plants) and Zoology (animals).


It also includes Microbiology—the study of microscopic organisms.


Biology helps us understand how our bodies work and how diseases can be prevented.


It plays a crucial role in medicine, agriculture, and environmental conservation.


Careers in biology include doctor, pharmacist, agriculturist, and laboratory scientist.


Biology uses scientific methods to investigate problems and find solutions.


Observations, experiments, and analysis are key in studying Biology.

        Living things are organisms that carry out life processes.


The basic characteristics of living things are summarized by MRS GREN.


M – Movement: Living things move either internally or externally.


R – Respiration: They release energy from food.


S – Sensitivity: They respond to stimuli in their environment.


G – Growth: Living things increase in size and complexity.


R – Reproduction: They produce offspring.


E – Excretion: They remove waste products from their bodies.


N – Nutrition: Living things take in and use nutrients.


These characteristics differentiate living things from non-living things.

        Plants and animals are both living things but have key differences.


Plants make their own food through photosynthesis; animals cannot.


Plants are usually rooted in one place; animals move around.


Plants have cell walls made of cellulose; animals do not.


Plants grow throughout their lives; animals grow to a certain stage.


Animals have complex nervous systems; plants do not.


Plants respond slowly to stimuli; animals respond quickly.


Reproduction in plants may involve seeds or spores; in animals, it usually involves birth or eggs.


Plants absorb nutrients mainly from soil; animals get theirs from food.


These differences reflect their adaptation to different lifestyles.

        The cell is the basic unit of life in all living organisms.


All living things are made up of cells.


Cells can be unicellular (one-celled) or multicellular (many-celled).


Robert Hooke discovered the cell in 1665 using a microscope.


There are plant cells and animal cells, each with specific features.


Common parts of a cell include the cell membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleus.


Plant cells have additional parts like cell wall and chloroplasts.


The nucleus controls the activities of the cell.


Cytoplasm is the jelly-like substance where reactions occur.


Cells work together to form tissues and organs.

        Human development is the process of physical, emotional, and mental changes from birth to adulthood.


It occurs in stages: infancy, childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and old age.


Infancy (0–2 years) involves rapid growth, development of senses, and basic motor skills.


Childhood (3–12 years) sees steady growth, language development, and learning social skills.


Adolescence (13–19 years) is marked by puberty, emotional changes, and independence.


Adulthood (20–59 years) includes peak physical strength and intellectual maturity.


Old age (60+) involves gradual decline in strength and health.


Each stage has different physical, emotional, and mental needs.


Balanced nutrition and care are essential at all stages.


Understanding development helps promote healthy living and support for all age groups.

        Nutrition is the process of taking in and using food for health and growth.


There are six main classes of food: carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water.


Carbohydrates give energy, while proteins help in growth and repair.


Fats store energy and keep the body warm.


Vitamins and minerals support body functions like immunity and bone strength.


Water is vital for digestion, temperature control, and waste removal.


A balanced diet contains the right amount of all food classes.


Malnutrition happens when the body lacks one or more nutrients.


Eating junk food often leads to poor health.


Good nutrition promotes growth, development, and disease prevention.

Commonly abused drugs include tobacco, alcohol, and marijuana.


Drug abuse can lead to addiction and serious health problems.


Smoking can damage the lungs and cause cancer.


Alcohol abuse can harm the liver and impair judgment.


Saying “NO” to drug offers is a healthy choice.


Education, support, and good friends help prevent drug abuse.

        All living organisms are made of cells — the basic unit of life.


Cells group together to form tissues that perform specific functions.


Tissues combine to form organs like the heart and lungs.


Organs work together in organ systems, such as the digestive or circulatory system.


Organ systems function together to form a complete organism.


A group of similar cells performing a job is called a tissue (e.g., muscle tissue).


An organ is made of different tissues (e.g., the stomach).


Organ systems coordinate body processes (e.g., excretion, movement).


The organization helps in efficiency and survival of the organism.


This structure is seen in both plants and animals.

        Specialized cells have unique structures for specific functions.


Examples include nerve cells, red blood cells, and root hair cells.


Nerve cells transmit signals in the body.


Red blood cells carry oxygen using haemoglobin.


Root hair cells absorb water and minerals in plants.


Muscle cells contract to cause movement.


Sperm cells are adapted to fertilize eggs with a tail for movement.


Cells become specialized through a process called differentiation.


This allows multicellular organisms to function effectively.


Each cell type is adapted for its job with distinct structures.

Land pollution results from improper waste disposal.


Noise pollution affects hearing and causes stress.


Pollution harms humans, animals, and plants.


It also leads to climate change and health issues.


Reducing pollution involves recycling, planting trees, and using clean energy.


Laws and awareness campaigns help reduce pollution.

Puberty is the stage when reproductive organs mature.


Hormones control changes in the reproductive system.

        STIs are infections passed from one person to another through sexual contact.


Common STIs include HIV/AIDS, gonorrhoea, syphilis, and chlamydia.


Symptoms may include sores, unusual discharge, pain during urination, or no symptoms at all.


HIV attacks the immune system and can lead to AIDS.


Gonorrhoea and syphilis are bacterial infections that can be treated with antibiotics.


STIs can be transmitted through vaginal, oral, or anal sex.


Using condoms can reduce the risk of getting or spreading STIs.


Abstinence and faithful relationships help prevent STIs.


Regular check-ups and early treatment prevent complications.


STIs can affect reproductive health and cause long-term damage if untreated.

        Invertebrates are animals without a backbone.


They make up over 95% of all animal species.


Major invertebrate groups include insects, arachnids, mollusks, annelids, and crustaceans.


Insects have 6 legs and 3 body parts – head, thorax, abdomen.


Arachnids like spiders have 8 legs and no antennae.


Mollusks (e.g., snails) have soft bodies, often with shells.


Annelids (e.g., earthworms) have segmented bodies.


Crustaceans (e.g., crabs) have hard exoskeletons and jointed limbs.


Invertebrates can live in land, water, or air environments.


They are classified based on body structure and movement.

        Vertebrates are animals with a backbone.


They belong to the phylum Chordata.


There are five major classes: fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.


Fish live in water and breathe through gills.


Amphibians live on land and water and have moist skin.


Reptiles have dry, scaly skin and lay eggs.


Birds have feathers and lay hard-shelled eggs.


Mammals have hair/fur and give birth to live young.


All vertebrates have a well-developed brain and skeleton.


Classification depends on body covering, reproduction, and movement.

        Classification keys help identify organisms.


A dichotomous key is a tool that uses two choices at each step.


"Dichotomous" means "divided into two parts."


The key works by comparing features of organisms.


Each step narrows down the choices.


The key can be presented as a list or a flowchart.


It uses observable features like size, shape, color.


Scientists use keys to name and classify species.


Accurate observation is needed for correct identification.


Keys are essential for studying biodiversity.

Aquatic habitats include freshwater and marine environments.


Freshwater habitats: rivers, lakes, ponds.


Marine habitats: seas, oceans, coral reefs.


Organisms adapt to survive in aquatic habitats.


Fish have gills to breathe in water.


Frogs have webbed feet to swim.


Some aquatic plants have air spaces to float.


Salinity, light, and temperature affect aquatic life.


Adaptation helps organisms feed, move, and reproduce

Thick fur or feathers help animals retain heat in cold areas.

Nocturnal animals stay active at night to avoid daytime heat.


Sharp claws or strong limbs help in climbing or digging.


Some plants have waxy leaves to reduce water loss.

Jackrabbits have large ears to release body heat.


Kangaroo rats can survive without drinking water.


Snakes and lizards hide under rocks during the day.


Plants have shallow roots to absorb rainfall quickly.


Adaptations in desert species reduce water loss.


These adaptations help organisms survive extreme heat and scarce water.
