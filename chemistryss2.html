The periodic table is a systematic arrangement of all known chemical elements in order of increasing atomic number. It is divided into periods (horizontal rows) and groups (vertical columns). Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence electrons.
The periodic table is classified into metals, non-metals, and metalloids. Metals are usually found on the left and center, non-metals on the right, and metalloids along a zig-zag line between them.
The periodic table is also divided into blocks (s, p, d, and f) based on the type of atomic orbital being filled. The alkali metals (Group 1) are highly reactive, especially with water, while alkaline earth metals (Group 2) are less reactive.
The transition metals (Groups 3–12) have variable oxidation states and form colored compounds. The halogens (Group 17) are reactive non-metals, and the noble gases (Group 18) are chemically inert.
The periodic table helps predict element behavior, atomic structure, and chemical reactivity.

Atomic properties such as atomic radius, ionization energy, electronegativity, and electron affinity vary across the periodic table in a predictable manner.
Atomic radius decreases across a period due to increased nuclear charge pulling electrons closer to the nucleus, but increases down a group due to the addition of electron shells.
Ionization energy is the energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms. It increases across a period and decreases down a group.
Electronegativity measures an atom’s tendency to attract shared electrons. It increases across a period and decreases down a group.
Electron affinity is the energy change when an atom gains an electron. Non-metals generally have high electron affinities.
These trends help explain the reactivity of metals (which lose electrons) and non-metals (which gain electrons). Alkali metals have low ionization energy, while halogens have high electronegativity.

An oxidation–reduction (redox) reaction involves the transfer of electrons between species. Oxidation is the loss of electrons or increase in oxidation number, while reduction is the gain of electrons or decrease in oxidation number.
In terms of oxygen and hydrogen, oxidation is the addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen, while reduction is the removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen.
Redox reactions occur in various processes such as respiration, photosynthesis, corrosion, and industrial metallurgy.
The oxidizing agent is the substance that causes oxidation by accepting electrons, while the reducing agent causes reduction by donating electrons.
Balancing redox equations can be done by ion–electron or oxidation number methods.

An oxidizing agent gains electrons and is reduced in the process. Common examples include KMnO₄, K₂Cr₂O₇, Cl₂, and H₂O₂.
A reducing agent loses electrons and is oxidized in the process. Examples include Zn, Fe, CO, and H₂.
To identify them in reactions, determine which species has an increase or decrease in oxidation number. The one whose oxidation number decreases is the oxidizing agent, while the one whose oxidation number increases is the reducing agent.
Strong oxidizing agents are usually non-metals or compounds containing high oxidation state elements, while strong reducing agents are typically metals or negative ions.
Their applications include bleaching, disinfection, metallurgy, and fuel combustion.

The ionic theory explains the behavior of substances in terms of ions. According to Arrhenius, electrolytes dissociate into positive (cations) and negative (anions) ions when dissolved in water or molten, enabling them to conduct electricity.
Ionic compounds like NaCl conduct electricity when molten or in solution, but not in solid state, because ions are fixed in a lattice.
Strong electrolytes dissociate completely (e.g., HCl, NaOH), while weak electrolytes dissociate partially (e.g., CH₃COOH, NH₃).
Electrolysis is the decomposition of an electrolyte by an electric current, leading to reactions at the anode (oxidation) and cathode (reduction).
The ionic theory is applied in electroplating, extraction of metals, and battery operation.

 Electrolysis is the chemical decomposition of an electrolyte by passing a direct electric current through it. An electrolyte is a compound (molten or in aqueous solution) that conducts electricity and undergoes chemical change. Electrolysis requires two electrodes: the cathode (negative, where reduction occurs) and the anode (positive, where oxidation occurs).
Electrodes may be inert (e.g., platinum, graphite) or active (participating in the reaction). The electrolyte contains positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions). Cations move to the cathode to gain electrons (reduction), while anions move to the anode to lose electrons (oxidation).
Electrolysis is widely used in electroplating, extraction of metals, purification of metals, and manufacture of chemicals like chlorine and sodium hydroxide. The products of electrolysis depend on the nature of the electrolyte, the electrodes, and the concentration of the solution.

The electrolysis of a given electrolyte depends on its ionic composition and the activity series of elements involved. For molten electrolytes like molten sodium chloride, only the ions from the compound are present, so sodium is deposited at the cathode and chlorine is liberated at the anode.
For aqueous electrolytes, water also ionizes to produce H⁺ and OH⁻ ions. The cation discharged at the cathode is determined by the position of the metal in the reactivity series — metals below hydrogen (e.g., copper, silver) are deposited, while above hydrogen, hydrogen gas is released. At the anode, halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are preferentially discharged over OH⁻, but if absent, oxygen gas is produced from OH⁻ ions.
Examples:
Aqueous copper(II) sulfate with copper electrodes: copper is deposited on the cathode, anode dissolves.


Dilute H₂SO₄: hydrogen is released at cathode, oxygen at anode.


Brine: hydrogen at cathode, chlorine at anode, NaOH in solution.

 An electrochemical cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy through redox reactions. It consists of two different electrodes immersed in electrolytes, connected by an external circuit and a salt bridge. The more reactive metal loses electrons (oxidation) at the anode, while the less reactive gains electrons (reduction) at the cathode.
Electrons flow from the more reactive to the less reactive metal through the external wire. The salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality by allowing ions to move between the half-cells. Common examples include the Daniell cell (zinc-copper) and dry cells used in torches.
The voltage produced depends on the difference in reactivity of the two metals. Cells are the basis of batteries and are important in powering portable devices.

Electromotive force (e.m.f) is the potential difference between two electrodes in an electrochemical cell when no current flows. It measures the cell’s ability to drive electrons through the external circuit. The e.m.f can be calculated from the standard electrode potentials (E°) of the two half-cells:
E°cell = E°cathode – E°anode
Electrode potentials are measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) which has an assigned potential of 0.00 V. A positive E°cell means the cell reaction is spontaneous. For example, in a zinc-copper cell: E°cathode (Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V, E°anode (Zn²⁺/Zn) = –0.76 V, so E°cell = 0.34 – (–0.76) = 1.10 V.
Knowledge of e.m.f calculations helps in predicting cell feasibility and designing batteries with desired voltages.

Faraday’s first law states that the mass (m) of a substance liberated or deposited during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (Q) passed through the electrolyte:
 m ∝ Q, where Q = I × t (I = current in amperes, t = time in seconds).
Faraday’s second law states that when the same quantity of electricity passes through different electrolytes, the masses of substances liberated are proportional to their chemical equivalent masses (E = M/z). One Faraday (F) = 96,500 coulombs is the charge required to deposit or liberate 1 mole of electrons.
Example: The mass of copper deposited when a current of 2.0 A flows for 30 minutes through CuSO₄ solution: Q = 2 × (30 × 60) = 3600 C, m = (63.5 × 3600) / (2 × 96500) = 1.18 g.

The rate of a chemical reaction is the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time. It can be expressed as: Rate = change in quantity / time taken. Reactions can be fast (e.g., explosions, precipitation) or slow (e.g., rusting, fermentation). The rate is determined by monitoring changes in mass, volume of gas produced, or colour change. At the molecular level, the rate depends on the frequency of collisions between reactant particles and the energy they possess. A higher collision frequency generally increases the rate. The rate can be represented graphically by plotting concentration against time, producing curves whose slopes represent speed. Chemical rates are important in industry to control costs, efficiency, and safety, ensuring optimal product yield in minimal time.

The mole is the standard unit for measuring the amount of a substance in chemistry. One mole is defined as the quantity containing 6.022 × 10²³ particles — atoms, molecules, or ions — and is based on the number of atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon-12.
The collision theory states that for a reaction to occur, reacting particles must collide with sufficient energy and correct orientation. This minimum required energy is called the activation energy (Eₐ).
Factors affecting rate include:
Concentration/pressure: Higher values increase collision frequency.
Temperature: Increases particle kinetic energy, leading to more collisions with energy ≥ Eₐ.
Surface area: Smaller particles provide more area for collision.
Catalysts: Lower the activation energy, increasing the rate without being consumed.
Nature of reactants: Ionic reactions occur faster than covalent due to simpler bond-breaking processes.

An energy profile diagram shows reactants, products, Eₐ, and the effect of catalysts.
Understanding these factors allows control of reaction rates in industrial and laboratory processes.

A chemical equilibrium occurs when the rates of forward and reverse reactions are equal, and concentrations of reactants and products remain constant. It is dynamic, not static.
Conditions for Equilibrium
 A chemical system reaches equilibrium when the rates of forward and reverse reactions are equal, and concentrations of reactants and products remain constant. It occurs in a closed system, with constant temperature and pressure. The system must be dynamic, meaning reactions continue at the molecular level.
Writing Equilibrium Constant (K꜀)
 For the reaction:
 N₂(g) + ³H₂(g) ⇌ ²NH₃(g)
K꜀ = [Product] / [Reactant]
 K꜀ = [NH₃]² / ([N₂] × [H₂]³)
Le Chatelier’s Principle states: if a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to oppose the change.
Factors affecting equilibrium:
Concentration: Increasing reactants shifts equilibrium to produce more products.
Temperature: For endothermic reactions, increasing temperature favours products; for exothermic, it favours reactants.
Pressure: In gaseous systems, increasing pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules.
Catalysts: Speed up attainment of equilibrium but do not change its position.

Equilibrium concepts are applied in the Haber process, contact process, and esterification.

The halogens are Group VII elements (Fluorine, Chlorine, Bromine, Iodine, and Astatine). They are highly reactive non-metals with seven valence electrons. Their reactivity decreases down the group, while boiling and melting points increase. Halogens exist as diatomic molecules (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂).
Chlorine is the most important halogen in industry. It is produced by the electrolysis of brine. Uses of chlorine include water purification, production of plastics (PVC), disinfectants, and bleaching agents.
Hydrogen halides (e.g., HCl, HBr, HI) are strong acids in aqueous solution, except HF which is weak due to strong H–F bonding. Silver salts of halides show characteristic precipitates with AgNO₃ (AgCl – white, AgBr – cream, AgI – yellow).

Nitrogen compounds are vital in nature, e.g., proteins and fertilizers. The nitrogen cycle ensures continuous supply of nitrogen to plants and animals through fixation, nitrification, and denitrification.

Important nitrogen compounds include ammonia, nitric acid, and oxides of nitrogen. Ammonia (NH₃) is a colorless gas with pungent odor, highly soluble in water forming ammonium hydroxide, an alkaline solution. It is prepared industrially by the Haber process.
Nitric acid (HNO₃) is a strong acid and powerful oxidizing agent. It is manufactured by the Ostwald process, using ammonia, oxygen, and a platinum catalyst. Nitric acid forms nitrates which are important fertilizers.
Oxides of nitrogen range from N₂O (laughing gas), NO (neutral), to NO₂ (acidic, toxic). Nitrogen compounds are important in agriculture, industry, and environmental pollution (acid rain, smog).

Sulphur is a yellow, brittle, non-metal found in volcanic regions and ores like galena. It is insoluble in water but soluble in carbon disulphide. It burns in air with a blue flame, forming SO₂.
Sulphur has allotropes: rhombic sulphur (stable below 96°C, crystalline, yellow), monoclinic sulphur (stable above 96°C, needle-like crystals), and plastic sulphur (obtained by pouring molten sulphur into cold water).
Extraction is mainly by the Frasch process, where superheated water and compressed air are forced into underground deposits, melting sulphur which is then pumped up. Sulphur is used in vulcanization of rubber, manufacture of sulphuric acid, matches, and fungicides.

 Water is one of the most important natural resources for life and industry. Its main sources include rainfall, rivers, lakes, springs, oceans (salty), and underground water. Natural water often contains impurities such as suspended particles, microorganisms, dissolved salts, and gases.
The treatment of water for town supply involves several processes. First, screening removes large objects. Sedimentation allows heavy particles to settle. Coagulation and flocculation with chemicals like alum help suspended matter clump and sink.
Next, filtration removes fine particles. Chlorination kills harmful microorganisms, while aeration improves taste and odor by dissolving oxygen. Finally, pH adjustment may be carried out to make the water safe and non-corrosive.
The treated water must be potable—safe for drinking, free from harmful bacteria, and pleasant to taste. Proper treatment ensures public health, prevents diseases like cholera and typhoid, and supplies industries with usable water.

Water that does not readily form lather with soap is called hard water, while water that forms lather easily is called soft water. Hardness is caused by dissolved calcium and magnesium salts.
There are two types of hardness: temporary hardness, due to dissolved calcium/magnesium bicarbonates, and permanent hardness, caused by sulphates and chlorides of calcium/magnesium. Temporary hardness can be removed by boiling or by adding slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂). Permanent hardness can be removed by ion-exchange resins, washing soda, or synthetic zeolites.
Soft water contains little or no dissolved calcium and magnesium salts. It is ideal for laundry and industrial processes where lather formation is required. Hard water, however, has advantages: it improves taste, provides essential minerals, and reduces pipe corrosion.
Excessive hardness causes scaling in kettles, boilers, and industrial machines, leading to inefficiency. Thus, water softening is important for both domestic and industrial use.

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in 100 g of solvent at a specific temperature. It is usually expressed in grams per 100 g of water. Solubility generally increases with temperature for most salts, but decreases for gases.
A solubility curve is a graph showing the solubility of a substance against temperature. The curve helps predict how much solute will dissolve at a given temperature. Points above the curve represent supersaturated solutions, while points below represent unsaturated solutions.
Solubility calculations involve determining mass of solute dissolved, mass of solvent used, and percentage solubility. For example, if 20 g of KNO₃ dissolves in 50 g of water at 40°C, the solubility is (20 ÷ 50) × 100 = 40 g per 100 g water.
Solubility curves are useful in crystallization, separation of salts, and industrial production of substances like sugar and fertilizers.

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures of two or more substances, consisting of a solute dissolved in a solvent. A true solution is transparent, particles are invisible to the naked eye, and they pass through filter paper (e.g., salt in water). A false solution appears like a solution but is unstable, separating on standing (e.g., oil in water). A suspension is a heterogeneous mixture where particles are visible, settle on standing, and can be filtered (e.g., chalk in water).
An aqueous solution is one in which water is the solvent. A saturated solution contains the maximum amount of solute the solvent can dissolve at a given temperature. An unsaturated solution contains less solute than it can dissolve, meaning more solute can still be added. A supersaturated solution is an unstable solution containing more solute than normally possible at that temperature; crystallization occurs easily if disturbed.
The type of solution depends on particle size, stability, and interaction with light. Colloids fall between true solutions and suspensions, showing the Tyndall effect. Understanding solution types helps in industries such as pharmaceuticals, food processing, and water treatment.

 Acid-base reactions are neutralization reactions in which acids react with bases to form salt and water. A typical example is HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O. Titration is a laboratory technique used to determine the concentration of an acid or base using a solution of known concentration.
In acid-base titration, an acid is added gradually from a burette into a measured volume of base (or vice versa) until complete neutralization is achieved. The point at which neutralization occurs is the equivalence point, usually detected by an indicator. Common indicators include methyl orange (red in acid, yellow in base) and phenolphthalein (colourless in acid, pink in base).
The measured volume of acid required to neutralize a given base is called the titre value. Repeated titrations are carried out to obtain consistent readings. Titrations are widely used in industries such as food, pharmaceuticals, and environmental monitoring to determine concentrations of solutions accurately.

Organic chemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies carbon compounds, especially those containing hydrogen. Hydrocarbons are the simplest class of organic compounds made up of carbon and hydrogen only. They form the backbone of most organic molecules.
Hydrocarbons are divided into aliphatic (straight or branched chains) and aromatic (ring structures with delocalized π-electrons). Aliphatic hydrocarbons are further classified into alkanes (saturated), alkenes (unsaturated with double bonds), and alkynes (unsaturated with triple bonds).
The bonding in hydrocarbons involves covalent sharing of electrons. Saturated hydrocarbons contain only single covalent bonds, while unsaturated hydrocarbons contain double or triple bonds. The general properties of hydrocarbons include combustibility, insolubility in water but solubility in organic solvents, and low boiling points compared to ionic compounds.
Hydrocarbons are very important as fuels (petrol, diesel, natural gas) and as raw materials in petrochemical industries for plastics, detergents, and drugs.

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only single covalent bonds between carbon atoms. They are also called paraffins due to their low reactivity. The general formula is CnH2n+2. Examples include methane, ethane, propane, and butane.
Alkanes exhibit tetrahedral geometry around carbon atoms due to sp³ hybridization. They undergo mainly substitution reactions, since addition reactions are not possible due to the absence of multiple bonds.
Physical properties of alkanes include insolubility in water, solubility in organic solvents, low boiling and melting points, and combustibility. Chemically, they undergo combustion, halogenation, and cracking.
Natural sources of alkanes include crude oil and natural gas. They are used as fuels, lubricants, and raw materials in petrochemical industries.

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond. Their general formula is CnH2n. The simplest alkene is ethene (C2H4).
Alkenes exhibit planar geometry around the double bond due to sp² hybridization. The double bond consists of a sigma (σ) bond and a pi (π) bond, which makes them more reactive than alkanes.
Physical properties are similar to alkanes: insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents, and combustible. Chemically, alkenes undergo addition reactions such as hydrogenation, halogenation, hydrohalogenation, and polymerization.
They are obtained from petroleum cracking and are used in making plastics, alcohols, and detergents.

Alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing at least one carbon-carbon triple bond. Their general formula is CnH2n-2. The simplest alkyne is ethyne (acetylene, C2H2).
Alkynes exhibit linear geometry due to sp hybridization. The triple bond consists of one sigma (σ) bond and two pi (π) bonds, making alkynes highly reactive.
Alkynes undergo addition reactions similar to alkenes, including hydrogenation, halogenation, and hydrohalogenation. They can also undergo combustion and polymerization.
Ethyne is industrially produced by the action of water on calcium carbide. Alkynes are important in welding (oxy-acetylene flame), manufacture of plastics, and as intermediates in organic synthesis.

Alkanols are organic compounds containing the hydroxyl (–OH) functional group attached to an alkyl chain. Their general formula is CnH2n+1OH. Examples include methanol, ethanol, and propanol.
Alkanols are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary depending on the position of the –OH group. They are polar compounds, soluble in water, and have higher boiling points than alkanes due to hydrogen bonding.
Chemically, alkanols undergo combustion, dehydration to form alkenes, esterification with carboxylic acids, and oxidation to aldehydes, ketones, or acids.
Ethanol is the most common alkanol, obtained from fermentation of sugars or petrochemical processes. Alkanols are important as fuels, solvents, disinfectants, and in the manufacture of perfumes and pharmaceuticals.
