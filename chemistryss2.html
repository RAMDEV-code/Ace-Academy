        Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas, each with distinct particle arrangements and properties. Solids have fixed shapes and volumes; liquids have fixed volumes but take the shape of their container; gases have neither fixed shape nor volume. 
Matter is made of tiny particles—atoms, molecules, or ions—held together by forces. Physical properties (colour, density, boiling point) and chemical properties (reactivity, flammability) describe matter. Matter can change state through melting, freezing, boiling, condensation, and sublimation, all influenced by temperature and pressure.

Atoms are the smallest units of matter that retain the properties of an element. They consist of three subatomic particles: protons (positively charged, found in the nucleus), neutrons (neutral, also in the nucleus), and electrons (negatively charged, orbiting the nucleus). 
The atomic number (Z) equals the number of protons, determining the element’s identity. The mass number (A) equals protons plus neutrons. Electrons occupy energy levels or shells, arranged according to the 2n² rule. The nucleus contains most of the atom’s mass, while electrons determine its chemical properties. Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. The modern atomic theory builds on Dalton’s ideas, refined by discoveries from Rutherford, Bohr, and quantum mechanics.

Relative Atomic Mass (Aᵣ) is the weighted average mass of an element’s atoms compared to 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom. It accounts for the natural abundance of isotopes. The unit used is atomic mass unit (amu), where 1 amu = 1/12 of the mass of carbon-12. 
To calculate Relative atomic mass, Aᵣ, multiply each isotope’s mass by its percentage abundance, sum the results, and divide by 100. For example, chlorine has isotopes Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%), giving Aᵣ = (35 × 75 + 37 × 25) ÷ 100 = 35.5. Relative Atomic Mass is dimensionless and used in chemical equations for molar calculations. It is different from atomic mass, which refers to the actual mass in kilograms.

        A compound is a pure substance formed when two or more elements chemically combine in a fixed ratio. The properties of a compound differ from those of its constituent elements. Compounds can be molecular (covalent) or ionic, depending on the type of bonding. Molecular compounds share electrons, while ionic compounds transfer electrons. 
The smallest unit of a molecular compound is a molecule, whereas that of an ionic compound is a formula unit. Compounds have definite chemical formulas and can only be separated into elements by chemical means. Examples include water (H₂O) and sodium chloride (NaCl). The law of definite proportions states that a chemical compound always contains the same proportion of elements by mass.

IUPAC nomenclature is a standardized system for naming chemical compounds to avoid ambiguity. 
Rules for Naming Compounds
1. Binary Compounds (Contain two different elements)
Name the metal (positive ion) first, then the non-metal (negative ion).
Change the ending of the non-metal to “-ide”.
If both elements are non-metals, use prefixes (mono-, di-, tri-, etc.) to show the number of atoms.
No prefix “mono-” for the first element’s name.
 Examples:
NaCl → Sodium chloride
CO₂ → Carbon dioxide


2. Ternary Compounds (Contain three or more different elements)
Often contain a polyatomic ion (e.g., SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻).
Name the metal (or positive ion) first, then the polyatomic ion as it appears.
If the metal has more than one charge, write its charge in Roman numerals.
For acids or bases, follow special naming rules (e.g., H₂SO₄ → Sulfuric acid).
 Examples:
KNO₃ → Potassium nitrate
FeSO₄ → Iron(II) sulfate


Complex compounds and organic compounds have their own systematic naming methods.

A mixture is a physical combination of two or more substances that are not chemically bonded. The components retain their individual properties and can be separated by physical methods such as filtration, distillation, evaporation, or chromatography. 
Mixtures can be homogeneous (uniform composition, e.g., salt solution) or heterogeneous (non-uniform composition, e.g., sand in water). Unlike compounds, mixtures have no fixed ratio of components. Alloys, such as brass and bronze, are examples of solid mixtures. Air is a gaseous mixture. The properties of mixtures depend on the properties of their constituents, and no chemical change occurs in their formation.
Some examples of mixtures and their constituents
Air – nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, water vapour, and other gases
Salt water – salt (sodium chloride) and water
Steel – iron and carbon (sometimes other metals like chromium)
Fruit salad – apples, bananas, oranges, grapes, and other fruits
Sand and salt – sand and salt
Oil and water – oil and water
Milk – water, fats, proteins, minerals, and sugars

        (Calculating Relative Molecular Mass, Percentage Composition by Mass, Empirical & Molecular Formula)
The mole is the amount of substance containing 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro’s number).
Relative molecular mass (Mᵣ) is the sum of the relative atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule.
Example: Mᵣ of H₂O = 2(1.0) + 16.0 = 18.0.
Percentage composition by mass:
 % by mass = (Mass of element ÷ Molar mass of compound) × 100.
Empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.
Steps to find empirical formula:
 ① Find mass (or % mass) of each element.
 ② Convert mass to moles.
 ③ Divide all mole values by the smallest mole value.
 ④ Multiply to get whole numbers if needed.
Molecular formula = Empirical formula × n, where n = Molar mass ÷ Empirical formula mass.
Empirical and molecular formulas are essential in chemical analysis.

The mole is the standard unit for measuring the amount of a substance in chemistry. One mole is defined as the quantity containing 6.022 × 10²³ particles — atoms, molecules, or ions — and is based on the number of atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon-12.
The number of moles (n) can be calculated from the relationship:
n = mass ÷ molar mass
For example, the molar mass of water (H₂O) is 18 g/mol. If we have 18 g of water, then:
n = 18 ÷ 18 = 1 mole

The mole–volume relationship applies to gases. At Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP), 1 mole of any gas occupies 22.4 dm³. This means that the volume of a gas can be calculated using:
V = n × 22.4
For example, 2 moles of oxygen gas (O₂) will occupy:
V = 2 × 22.4 = 44.8 dm³

Moles can also be determined from the number of particles using:
n = number of particles ÷ 6.022 × 10²³
For example, 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules of carbon dioxide (CO₂) correspond to:
n = (1.204 × 10²⁴) ÷ (6.022 × 10²³) = 2 moles

 A balanced chemical equation gives mole ratios; these ratios, combined with mass or volume conversions, allow calculation of moles of reactants or products.
 Procedure: 
① Write and balance the equation; 
② Convert known mass/volume to moles (n = mass ÷ Mr or n = volume ÷ 22.4 dm³ at STP); 
③ Use mole ratio from the equation to find unknown moles; 
④ Convert moles back to mass or volume if required.
Example 1 — Mass to mass: 8.0 g H₂ reacts with excess O₂ to form H₂O; 
balanced equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.
 ① Step 1: Convert H₂ mass to moles: Mr(H₂) = 2.0, n(H₂) = 8.0 ÷ 2.0 = 4.0 mol.
 ② Step 2: Use mole ratio from the equation: 2 mol H₂ produce 2 mol H₂O, so n(H₂O) = 4.0 mol.
 ③ Step 3: Convert moles H₂O to mass: Mr(H₂O) = 18.0, mass = n × Mr = 4.0 × 18.0 = 72.0 g; therefore 72.0 g H₂O formed.
Example 2 — Mass to volume (gas at STP): 2.00 g C burns in excess O₂ to give CO₂; 
balanced equation: C + O₂ → CO₂.
 ① Step 1: Convert carbon mass to moles: Mr(C) = 12.0, n(C) = 2.00 ÷ 12.0 = 0.1667 mol.
 ② Step 2: Mole ratio 1 C : 1 CO₂ so n(CO₂) = 0.1667 mol and volume at STP = n × 22.4 dm³ = 0.1667 × 22.4 = 3.73 dm³ (to three s.f.).

Gay-Lussac’s Law of Combining Volumes states that when gases react together at the same temperature and pressure, the volumes of the reacting gases and the volumes of the gaseous products (if gaseous) are in simple whole number ratios. These ratios are determined directly from the balanced chemical equation. This law is useful in calculating residual gases and unreacted gases in reactions where one reactant is in excess.
Example 1 – Calculating Residual Gas
 Reaction: H2 + Cl2→2HCl
Balanced equation shows 1 volume of H2H₂H2​ reacts with 1 volume of Cl₂​.
Suppose 50 cm³ of H₂​ is mixed with 80 cm³ of Cl₂​
 From the ratio 1:1, 50 cm³ of H₂​ will react with 50 cm³ of Cl₂​.
The H₂​ is completely used, leaving 80−50 = 30 80 - 50 = 30 Cl₂​ unreacted.
Total gas after reaction = unreacted Cl2Cl₂Cl2​ + 2 volumes of HCl formed = 30 cm³ Cl₂​ + 100 cm³ HCl = 130 cm³.
Example 2 – Calculating Unreacted Gas and Residual Volume
 Reaction: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Balanced equation shows 2 volumes of H₂​ react with 1 volume of O₂​.
Suppose 60 cm³ of H₂​ is mixed with 60 cm³ of O₂​.
From the ratio 2:1, 60 cm³ of H₂​ requires 30 cm³ of O₂​.
This leaves 60 − 30 = 30cm³ of O₂​ unreacted.
Since H₂O is liquid at room temperature, total residual gas = 30 cm³ of O₂​ only.

Chemical bonding refers to the force that holds atoms or ions together in compounds. Atoms combine to achieve a stable electronic configuration, often a noble gas configuration, through the transfer or sharing of electrons. There are different types of bonds, with ionic bonding being one of the most common.
Ionic bonding occurs when electrons are completely transferred from one atom to another, usually between metals and non-metals. Metals lose electrons to form positively charged ions (cations), while non-metals gain electrons to form negatively charged ions (anions). The electrostatic attraction between these oppositely charged ions forms the ionic bond.
A classic example is sodium chloride (NaCl). Sodium (Na) has an electronic configuration of 2,8,1 and loses one electron to chlorine (Cl), which has 2,8,7, forming Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions. These ions are held together in a giant lattice structure.
Properties of ionic compounds include high melting and boiling points due to strong electrostatic forces, solubility in polar solvents like water, and electrical conductivity in molten or aqueous states but not in solid state.

Covalent bonding occurs when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons to achieve stability. This bond usually occurs between non-metal atoms with similar electronegativities. The shared electrons are attracted to the nuclei of both atoms.
There are single, double, and triple covalent bonds. A single bond involves one shared pair of electrons (e.g., H₂), a double bond has two shared pairs (e.g., O₂), and a triple bond has three shared pairs (e.g., N₂). Each shared pair is represented by a line in structural formulas.
Examples include water (H₂O), where oxygen shares electrons with two hydrogen atoms, and carbon dioxide (CO₂), where carbon forms double bonds with each oxygen atom.
Properties of covalent compounds include low melting and boiling points due to weak intermolecular forces, poor electrical conductivity (since no free ions exist), and varied solubility — they may dissolve in non-polar solvents but not in water.

The kinetic theory of matter states that matter is made up of particles (atoms or molecules) in constant motion. For gases, this motion explains gas laws.
Boyle’s Law states that for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to its volume:
 P₁V₁ = P₂V₂.
Example: A gas has a volume of 200 cm³ at a pressure of 100 kPa. What is its volume at 250 kPa?
 Solution: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
 100 × 200 = 250 × V₂
 V₂ = (100 × 200) / 250 = 80 cm³.
Charles’s Law states that at constant pressure, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (K):
 V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂.
Example: A gas at 27°C (300 K) occupies 500 cm³. What is its volume at 77°C (350 K)?
 Solution: V₂ = (500 × 350) / 300 = 583.3 cm³.

 The general gas equation combines Boyle’s and Charles’s laws:
 (P₁V₁) / T₁ = (P₂V₂) / T₂.
 It is used when both temperature and pressure change for a fixed mass of gas.
Example: A gas occupies 400 cm³ at 100 kPa and 27°C (300 K). What is its volume at 80 kPa and 77°C (350 K)?
 Solution:
 (100 × 400) / 300 = (80 × V₂) / 350
 (40000 / 300) = (80 × V₂) / 350
 133.33 = (80 × V₂) / 350
 80 × V₂ = 46666.67
 V₂ = 583.33 cm³.
The ideal gas equation is PV = nRT, where:
 P = pressure (Pa), V = volume (m³), n = moles, R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹K⁻¹, T = temperature (K).
Example: Calculate the pressure exerted by 2 moles of gas in a 5 L container at 300 K.
 Solution: V = 0.005 m³
 P = (nRT) / V = (2 × 8.314 × 300) / 0.005 = 997,680 Pa or 997.68 kPa.

Graham’s Law states that the rate of diffusion of a gas is inversely proportional to the square root of its molar mass:
 Rate₁ / Rate₂ = √(M₂ / M₁).
This means lighter gases diffuse faster than heavier gases. Diffusion is the spreading of gas particles from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration due to random motion.
Example: Compare the rate of diffusion of hydrogen (M = 2 g/mol) to oxygen (M = 32 g/mol).
 Rate_H₂ / Rate_O₂ = √(32 / 2) = √16 = 4.
 So, hydrogen diffuses four times faster than oxygen.
Example: If gas A has a molar mass of 44 g/mol and gas B has a molar mass of 4 g/mol,
 Rate_A / Rate_B = √(4 / 44) = √(0.0909) ≈ 0.302.
 Gas B diffuses about 3.31 times faster than gas A.
Graham’s Law is important in separating isotopes and in effusion studies.

 Acids are substances that produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water. They have a sour taste and turn blue litmus paper red. Common examples include hydrochloric acid (HCl), sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), and citric acid.
Acids are classified into strong acids (completely ionize in water, e.g., HCl) and weak acids (partially ionize, e.g., ethanoic acid). They can be organic (carbon-based) or inorganic (mineral acids).
Acids react with metals to produce salt and hydrogen gas, with carbonates to produce salt, carbon dioxide, and water, and with alkalis to form salts and water (neutralization).
The strength of an acid depends on its degree of ionization. Acids are measured on the pH scale, with values less than 7. Concentrated acids are corrosive and must be handled with care.
Acids are used in industry for manufacturing fertilizers, dyes, batteries, and cleaning agents.

Bases are substances that react with acids to form salts and water. They are proton acceptors according to the Brønsted-Lowry definition. Alkalis are bases that are soluble in water and produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻).
Common bases include sodium hydroxide (NaOH), calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂), and ammonia (NH₃). Alkalis include NaOH, KOH, and Ca(OH)₂.
Bases feel slippery to touch and turn red litmus paper blue. They can be strong (fully ionize in water) or weak (partially ionize).
Bases react with acids in neutralization reactions, with ammonium salts to release ammonia gas, and with some metals to form complex compounds.
They are used in making soap, paper, cleaning agents, and neutralizing acidic soils. Some bases, such as quicklime, are used in building.

 Salts are ionic compounds formed when the hydrogen ion in an acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion. Examples include sodium chloride (NaCl), copper(II) sulfate (CuSO₄), and ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃).
Salts are classified as normal salts, acid salts, basic salts, and double salts depending on their composition. They may be soluble (e.g., NaNO₃) or insoluble (e.g., BaSO₄).
Salts can be prepared by neutralization, precipitation, or direct combination of elements. The preparation method depends on the solubility of the salt.
Common properties of salts include high melting points, electrical conductivity in molten or aqueous states, and crystalline structure.
Uses of salts include food preservation (NaCl), fertilizers (NH₄NO₃), and electroplating (AgNO₃).

Acidity and alkalinity describe the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in a solution. The pH scale, ranging from 0 to 14, measures this concentration.
Solutions with pH less than 7 are acidic, pH equal to 7 is neutral, and pH greater than 7 is alkaline. Strong acids have pH close to 0, while strong alkalis have pH close to 14.
Indicators such as litmus, methyl orange, and phenolphthalein are used to determine acidity or alkalinity. Universal indicator and pH meters provide more accurate measurements.
Acidity in soils can affect crop yield; liming with CaO or Ca(OH)₂ can reduce acidity. Water bodies with extreme pH values can harm aquatic life.
Control of acidity and alkalinity is essential in industries such as food processing, pharmaceuticals, and water treatment.

Carbon exists in several physical forms called allotropes, which have different physical properties but are made of the same element. The most common crystalline allotropes are diamond, graphite, and fullerene. Diamond is transparent, extremely hard, and a poor conductor of electricity. Graphite is black, soft, slippery, and a good conductor of electricity due to delocalized electrons.
Amorphous allotropes include coal, coke, lampblack, charcoal, and carbon black. These forms lack a regular crystal structure. Diamond is used for cutting tools and jewellery, graphite in lubricants, electrodes, and pencil leads.
The properties of carbon allotropes are due to differences in bonding: diamond has a tetrahedral network of covalent bonds, while graphite has layers of hexagonal carbon atoms held by weak van der Waals forces. Fullerenes (like C₆₀) are spherical molecules with unique nanotechnology uses.
Carbon’s allotropy explains its wide range of industrial applications, from construction to electronics and medicine.

Carbon forms two main oxides: carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon dioxide (CO₂).
Carbon monoxide is a colorless, odorless, and poisonous gas produced by incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels. It binds strongly to hemoglobin, preventing oxygen transport in the body.
Carbon dioxide is a colorless, odorless gas produced by complete combustion of carbon, respiration, and decomposition. It is non-toxic but can cause suffocation in high concentrations. It is used in fire extinguishers, carbonated drinks, and as a refrigerant in solid form (dry ice).
CO is a reducing agent in metallurgy, while CO₂ is an oxidizing agent in photosynthesis. Both gases play roles in environmental chemistry: CO is a pollutant, and CO₂ is a greenhouse gas linked to climate change.

Hydrocarbons are compounds made of carbon and hydrogen only. They are classified into alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes.
Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only single bonds, with the general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. They are relatively unreactive but undergo combustion and substitution reactions. Examples include methane, ethane, and propane.
Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons with at least one double bond, formula CₙH₂ₙ. They are more reactive and undergo addition reactions, making them important in polymer production.
Alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons with at least one triple bond, formula CₙH₂ₙ₋₂. They are highly reactive and used in welding (ethyne).
Hydrocarbons occur naturally in crude oil and natural gas, serving as fuels and raw materials for the petrochemical industry.

Hydrocarbons can also be classified into aliphatic and aromatic compounds.
Aliphatic hydrocarbons include straight or branched chains (alkanes, alkenes, alkynes). Aromatic hydrocarbons contain one or more benzene rings, with delocalized electrons giving them special stability.
Benzene (C₆H₆) is a colorless liquid with a characteristic odor, used in making dyes, detergents, plastics, and drugs. It is toxic and carcinogenic, requiring careful handling.
Reactions of aromatic hydrocarbons differ from aliphatic ones; benzene undergoes substitution rather than addition due to its stable ring.
Crude oil refining produces various hydrocarbon fractions like petrol, kerosene, and diesel, obtained by fractional distillation. These are vital for energy and industrial processes.

Chemical industries are grouped into fine and heavy chemical industries. Fine chemicals are pure, single chemical substances produced in small quantities for specific uses, e.g., pharmaceuticals, food additives, and dyes. Heavy chemicals are produced in large quantities, often of lower purity, for industrial use, e.g., sulphuric acid, sodium hydroxide, and ammonia.
Raw materials for chemical industries include air, water, limestone, petroleum, and minerals. The choice of industry location depends on proximity to raw materials, energy, transport, market, and labour.
Heavy chemical products are used in manufacturing detergents, fertilisers, and plastics. Fine chemicals are used in medicine, electronics, and high-tech industries.
Environmental concerns include pollution, waste disposal, and energy consumption. Sustainable practices involve recycling, pollution control, and energy efficiency.
