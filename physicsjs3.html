A simple machine is a device that makes work easier by multiplying force or changing its direction. Common examples include lever, pulley, wheel and axle, inclined plane, wedge, and screw. The main function is to reduce the effort needed to do work. 
A lever consists of a rigid bar pivoted on a fixed point called the fulcrum. Levers are classified into first, second, and third class based on the position of fulcrum, load, and effort.
The pulley helps lift heavy loads using ropes or chains, and can be fixed or movable.
The wheel and axle consist of a wheel attached to a smaller axle to reduce friction.
Inclined planes allow heavy loads to be raised with less effort over a longer distance.
The wedge and screw are variations of the inclined plane used to split or fasten objects.
Simple machines help in saving energy, time, and effort in work.

In a closed circuit, current flows; in an open circuit, it stops.
Electrical symbols are used to represent components in circuit diagrams.
Series connection means components are connected one after another. While in series circuits, if one component fails, the whole circuit stops working.

Reflection is the bouncing back of light from a surface.
The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.
Regular reflection occurs on smooth surfaces, producing clear images. Diffuse reflection occurs on rough surfaces, scattering light in many directions.


Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another. Refraction occurs due to a change in the speed of light in different media. Light bends towards the normal when it enters a denser medium. Light bends away from the normal when it enters a less dense medium.
Reflection allows us to see images in mirrors. Refraction explains phenomena like the apparent bending of a stick in water.

Violet bends the most, while red bends the least. Rainbows are natural examples of dispersion in the atmosphere. Water droplets in the air act like prisms to split sunlight. The study of light colours is called spectroscopy.
Isaac Newton first demonstrated dispersion using a glass prism.

Magnetism is the force of attraction or repulsion produced by magnets. A magnet has two poles: North pole and South pole. Like poles repel each other while unlike poles attract.
Magnetic materials include iron, cobalt, and nickel. Non-magnetic materials include wood, plastic, and rubber.


The region around a magnet where its force can be felt is called a magnetic field.
Magnetic field lines flow from the North pole to the South pole outside the magnet.
Magnets can be natural (like lodestone) or artificial (made by humans).
A temporary magnet loses its magnetism quickly, while a permanent magnet retains it for a long time.
Magnetism has uses in compasses, electric motors, magnetic storage devices, and door latches.

Motion is the change in position of a body with time, relative to a reference point. If a body remains in the same position, it is said to be at rest.
There are two broad types of motion: translational motion and rotational motion. Translational motion occurs when a body moves from one point to another in space. This may be in a straight line or along a curved path.
Linear motion is translational motion along a straight path, while curvilinear motion occurs along a curved path. Examples include a car moving on a straight road and a ball thrown in the air.
Rotational motion occurs when a body turns about a fixed axis, e.g., a ceiling fan blade or the Earthâ€™s rotation about its axis.
Periodic motion is motion that repeats itself at equal time intervals, e.g., the vibration of a pendulum or the oscillation of a tuning fork.
Random motion is irregular, unpredictable motion, such as the movement of dust particles in the air.
In summary, motion can be classified as linear, curvilinear, rotational, periodic, or random depending on the nature of the path and repetition.
