Scalars and Vectors are two categories of physical quantities.
 A scalar quantity has magnitude only, without direction. Examples include distance, mass, time, speed, and temperature.
 A vector quantity has both magnitude and direction. Examples include displacement, velocity, acceleration, momentum, and force.
The main difference is that scalars are fully described by size alone, while vectors require both size and direction. Scalars are written in ordinary symbols, while vectors are written in bold or with arrows (e.g., v or →v).
Addition of Scalars is straightforward: they are added or subtracted algebraically. For example, if a student walks 5 m and then 7 m, the total distance is 12 m.
Addition of Vectors depends on direction. If two forces act in the same direction, they add up; if in opposite directions, they subtract. For instance, 20 N right and 5 N left give a resultant of 15 N right.
Relative velocity describes how one object appears to move relative to another. Its formula is:
 VAB = VA − VB.
 When two objects move in the same direction, relative velocity is the difference. When in opposite directions, it is the sum.
Resolution of vectors means breaking a vector into perpendicular components (horizontal and vertical). This simplifies calculations in mechanics.
The parallelogram law of vectors states: If two vectors are represented by adjacent sides of a parallelogram, their resultant is given by the diagonal.
Mathematically, if two vectors A and B are inclined at angle θ, the resultant R is:
 R = √(A² + B² + 2ABcosθ).
The direction α of the resultant is given by:
 tan α = (B sinθ) / (A + B cosθ).
Example: If two forces of 6 N and 8 N act at 90°, the resultant is √(6² + 8²) = 10 N, at an angle tan⁻¹(8/6) ≈ 53°.
Thus, vector analysis helps in solving problems involving displacement, velocity, force, and other directional quantities.

Rectilinear motion is motion along a straight line. The main quantities involved are speed, velocity, and acceleration.
Speed (v):
 The rate of change of distance with time. It is a scalar quantity.
   v = d ÷ t   where d = distance (m), t = time (s).
Velocity (v):
 The rate of change of displacement with time. It is a vector quantity.
   v = x ÷ t   where x = displacement (m), t = time (s).
Acceleration (a):
 The rate of change of velocity with time.
   a = (v − u) ÷ t    where u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, t = time.
From the definition of acceleration, the first equation of motion is:
   v = u + a·t
The second equation of motion is derived from displacement:
   s = u·t + ½·a·t²
The third equation of motion is obtained by eliminating time:
   v² = u² + 2·a·s
Another useful expression is:
   s = (u + v) ÷ 2 × t
These equations hold only under uniform acceleration.
For motion under gravity, acceleration a is replaced by g (≈ 9.8 m/s²). If the body is moving upward, g is taken as negative; if downward, positive.
Examples:
Example 1: A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 2.5 m/s² for 12 s.  Find: final velocity (v) and displacement (s).
Given: u = 0 m/s, a = 2.5 m/s², t = 12 s
Step 1 (final velocity):
  v = u + a·t
  v = 0 + (2.5)·12 = 30 m/s
Step 2 (displacement):
  s = u·t + ½·a·t²
  s = 0·12 + ½·(2.5)·12²
  s = 1.25·144 = 180 m
Answer: v = 30 m/s, s = 180 m.
Example 2: A bike slows from 18 m/s to 6 m/s uniformly in 4 s.  Find: acceleration (a) and displacement (s).
Given: u = 18 m/s, v = 6 m/s, t = 4 s
Step 1 (acceleration):
  a = (v − u) ÷ t
  a = (6 − 18) ÷ 4 = (−12) ÷ 4 = −3 m/s²
Step 2 (displacement via average velocity):
  s = (u + v) ÷ 2 × t
  s = (18 + 6) ÷ 2 × 4
  s = 24 ÷ 2 × 4 = 12 × 4 = 48 m
(Check with v² = u² + 2·a·s):
  6² = 18² + 2·(−3)·s ⇒ 36 = 324 − 6s ⇒ 6s = 324 − 36 = 288 ⇒ s = 48 ✓
Answer: a = −3 m/s², s = 48 m.
Example 3: A stone is thrown vertically upward at 32 m/s. Take g = 9.8 m/s² downward.
 Find: time to reach maximum height (t↑), maximum height (H), and total time of flight (T).
Given: u = 32 m/s (upward), a = −g = −9.8 m/s² at all times upward.

Sir Isaac Newton formulated three fundamental laws of motion.
Newton’s First Law (Law of Inertia): A body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force. This explains why a passenger jerks forward when a car suddenly stops.
Newton’s Second Law: The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and occurs in the direction of the force. Mathematically:
F = ma
where F is force, m is mass, and a is acceleration.
Newton’s Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. For example, a rocket moves upward because gases are expelled downward.
Momentum is defined as the product of mass and velocity:
p=mv
Impulse is the product of force and time:
Impulse = Ft

These laws explain everyday phenomena such as walking, driving, swimming, and flying.
Applications include the design of vehicles, aircraft, sports science, and space exploration.

Equilibrium of forces occurs when the resultant force acting on a body is zero. There are two main types: static equilibrium, when a body is at rest, and dynamic equilibrium, when a body moves at constant velocity without acceleration.
The conditions for equilibrium are: (1) the algebraic sum of all forces in any direction must be zero, and (2) the algebraic sum of moments of all forces about any point must be zero.
The moment of a force is the turning effect produced by a force acting at a distance from a pivot. It is defined as:
 Moment = Force × Perpendicular distance from pivot.
The principle of moments states that: For a body in equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments about a point equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about the same point.
This principle is applied in levers, balances, cranes, and bridges. The concept of couple (two equal and opposite parallel forces) also relates to rotational equilibrium.
Example 1: Uniform metre rule pivoted at the 40 cm mark. A 0.30 kg mass hangs at the 20 cm mark (left). What mass mmm at the 70 cm mark (right) will balance it?
Distances from pivot
 Left arm = 40 − 20 = 20 cm
 Right arm = 70 − 40 = 30 cm
Moments about the pivot (clockwise = anticlockwise)
 0.30 × 20 = m × 30
 6.0 = 30m
 m = 6.0 / 30 = 0.20 kg
Answer: Hang 0.20 kg at the 70 cm mark.

The centre of gravity (C.G.) of a body is the point through which its entire weight appears to act, regardless of the body’s orientation. For uniform regular bodies, the C.G. lies at the geometric centre. For irregular bodies, it can be found by the plumb-line method.
Stability of objects refers to the tendency of a body to return to its original position after being displaced slightly. There are three types:
Stable equilibrium – when the object returns to its original position after displacement.
Unstable equilibrium – when the object moves further away after displacement.
Neutral equilibrium – when the object remains in its new position after displacement.
The stability of an object depends on: (1) the height of its centre of gravity, (2) the size of its base, and (3) its shape.
Practical applications include vehicle design (low centre of gravity for cars), building foundations (broad base for stability), and sports (athletes lower their centre of gravity for balance).

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a type of periodic motion in which the acceleration of a body is directly proportional to its displacement from a fixed point and directed towards that point. Examples include a simple pendulum, mass-spring system, and vibrating strings.
The defining equation of SHM is:
 a∝−x, where a is acceleration and x is displacement.
The motion is sinusoidal in nature, usually described using sine or cosine functions. The period (T) is the time taken for one complete oscillation, while frequency (f) is the number of oscillations per second, with f=1/T​.
For a spring, the period is given by T=2π√m/k ​​, where m is mass and k is spring constant. For a simple pendulum, T=2π√l/g, where l is length and g is acceleration due to gravity.
In SHM, maximum displacement from the mean position is called amplitude, while the restoring force is responsible for the motion. Energy alternates between kinetic and potential, but the total energy remains constant in the absence of damping.

In continuation, we study the energy changes and graphical representation in SHM. At the extreme positions, potential energy is maximum and kinetic energy is zero. At the mean position, kinetic energy is maximum while potential energy is zero.
The displacement-time graph of SHM is sinusoidal, while the velocity-time graph is also sinusoidal but out of phase by 90°. The acceleration-time graph is also sinusoidal but 180° out of phase with displacement.
Damping occurs when resistive forces such as friction or air resistance reduce the amplitude of oscillation. In under-damping, oscillations gradually decrease; in critical damping, the system returns to equilibrium quickly without oscillating; in over-damping, it returns very slowly.
Resonance happens when the frequency of an external force matches the natural frequency of a system, causing large amplitude oscillations. While resonance can be useful (e.g., in radio tuning), it can also be destructive (e.g., bridge collapse).
SHM is important in real life, such as in clocks, musical instruments, seismology, and alternating current circuits.

Heat is a form of energy transferred due to temperature difference. The amount of heat a body absorbs depends on its mass, material, and temperature rise.
 The heat capacity (C) of a body is the quantity of heat required to raise its temperature by 1 kelvin (or 1°C).
 Mathematically:
 𝐶 = 𝑄 ÷ Δ𝜃
 where 𝑄 = heat absorbed, Δ𝜃 = temperature rise.
Heat capacity depends on the substance and its mass. A large mass has higher heat capacity.

Gas laws describe the relationship between the pressure (P), volume (V), and temperature (T) of a fixed mass of gas. These relationships are derived from experiments and are essential in understanding gas behavior.
① Boyle’s Law
 At constant temperature, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to its pressure.
𝑷 ∝ 𝟏/𝑽   or  𝑷𝑽 = 𝒌
Example ① (Boyle’s Law):

𝑷₁𝑽₁ = 𝑷₂𝑽₂
 𝟏𝟎𝟎 × 𝟐𝟎𝟎 = 𝟐𝟓𝟎 × 𝑽₂
 𝑽₂ = 𝟐𝟎𝟎𝟎𝟎 / 𝟐𝟓𝟎 = 𝟖𝟎 𝐜𝐦³
② Charles’ Law
 At constant pressure, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.
𝑽 / 𝑻 = 𝒌   (𝑻 in Kelvin)
Example ② (Charles’ Law):
 A gas has a volume of 𝟑𝟎𝟎 𝐜𝐦³ at 𝟐𝟕 ℃. Find its volume at 𝟏𝟐𝟕 ℃ (pressure constant).
𝑻₁ = 𝟐𝟕 + 𝟐𝟕𝟑 = 𝟑𝟎𝟎 𝐊
 𝑻₂ = 𝟏𝟐𝟕 + 𝟐𝟕𝟑 = 𝟒𝟎𝟎 𝐊
𝑽₁ / 𝑻₁ = 𝑽₂ / 𝑻₂
 𝟑𝟎𝟎 / 𝟑𝟎𝟎 = 𝑽₂ / 𝟒𝟎𝟎
 𝑽₂ = 𝟒𝟎𝟎 𝐜𝐦³
③ Pressure Law
 At constant volume, the pressure of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.
𝑷 / 𝑻 = 𝒌
④ General Gas Law
 By combining Boyle’s, Charles’ and Pressure laws:
(𝑷₁𝑽₁) / 𝑻₁ = (𝑷₂𝑽₂) / 𝑻₂

Curved mirrors are spherical mirrors whose reflecting surface is part of a sphere. They are of two types: concave (converging) and convex (diverging).
Terms Used in Curved Mirrors
Pole (P): The center of the mirror’s surface.
Centre of curvature (C): The center of the sphere from which the mirror is cut.
Radius of curvature (R): Distance between C and P.
Principal axis: The line passing through P and C.
Principal focus (F): The point where parallel rays converge (concave) or appear to diverge (convex).
Focal length (f): Distance between F and P.

Dispersion of light is the splitting of white light into its component colours when it passes through a transparent medium such as a glass prism. White light is made up of seven colours – red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet – collectively called the spectrum.
This phenomenon occurs because different colours of light travel at different speeds in a medium other than air or vacuum. The bending of light, known as refraction, depends on its wavelength; shorter wavelengths (like violet) bend more, while longer wavelengths (like red) bend less.
A triangular glass prism is commonly used to demonstrate dispersion. When a narrow beam of sunlight is allowed to pass through the prism, the emergent light forms a band of colours on a screen. This confirms that white light is a mixture of several colours.
Dispersion should not be confused with refraction. While refraction is simply the bending of light, dispersion involves both bending and separation of colours due to their different refractive indices in the medium.
Applications of dispersion include rainbow formation in nature, spectrometer analysis in laboratories, and the working principle of diffraction gratings used in optical instruments. Understanding dispersion helps in studying wave optics and the wave nature of light.

Sound waves are mechanical waves that require a medium (air, liquid, or solid) for transmission. They are produced by vibrating bodies and travel as longitudinal waves consisting of compressions and rarefactions.
The speed of sound depends on the medium: slowest in gases, faster in liquids, and fastest in solids. In air at 20°C, the speed is about 340 m/s.
The pitch of a sound depends on its frequency, loudness depends on amplitude, and quality (timbre) distinguishes two sounds of the same pitch and loudness.
The audible range for humans is 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Frequencies below 20 Hz are infrasonic, while those above 20,000 Hz are ultrasonic.
Sound intensity is power per unit area, decreasing with distance from the source. The decibel (dB) scale measures sound level relative to a standard reference intensity.

Sound waves exhibit reflection, refraction, and diffraction. Echoes occur when sound reflects from a hard surface like a wall. For a clear echo, the reflecting surface must be at least 17 m away.
Refraction of sound occurs due to changes in air temperature or density, while diffraction makes sound bend around obstacles and pass through small openings.
Interference of sound produces beats when two sources of close frequencies combine. Beat frequency equals the difference in their frequencies.
The Doppler effect is the apparent change in sound frequency due to relative motion between source and observer. Approaching sources increase frequency, while receding ones decrease it.
Sound is applied in SONAR, echolocation (bats), and ultrasound in medicine.

Resonance occurs when a vibrating system is forced to oscillate with maximum amplitude at its natural frequency.
Examples include vibrating strings, air columns in pipes, and tuning forks. Resonance may be useful (musical instruments) or destructive (collapsed bridges).
The fundamental frequency of a vibrating string depends on its length, tension, and mass per unit length. In air columns, resonance depends on pipe length and whether it is open or closed.
Resonance explains why soldiers break step on bridges, and why glass may shatter at a sound’s natural frequency.
