A chemical reaction is a process where substances change to form new substances. In an exothermic reaction, heat energy is released to the surroundings. Examples: combustion of fuel, neutralization of acid and base, respiration. Exothermic reactions make the surroundings warmer. In an endothermic reaction, heat energy is absorbed from the surroundings. Examples: photosynthesis, dissolving salt in water, thermal decomposition. Endothermic reactions make the surroundings cooler. Energy changes in reactions can be measured using a thermometer. The energy change determines whether a reaction is exothermic or endothermic. Understanding these reactions is important in industry and everyday life.

Several factors can speed up or slow down a chemical reaction. These factors include:
Temperature: higher temperature increases particle movement, speeding up reactions.
Concentration: higher concentration means more particles, leading to more collisions.
Surface area: smaller particle size exposes more surface, increasing reaction rate.
Catalyst: a substance that speeds up reaction without being used up.
Pressure: in gases, higher pressure forces particles closer, increasing collisions.
Removing heat can slow down a reaction. Lower concentration slows down reaction rate.
Catalysts are important in industry to save time and energy. Each factor affects how often and how energetically particles collide.

Giant molecules are substances made up of a huge number of atoms bonded together in a regular arrangement.
Diamond and graphite are examples of giant covalent structures made of carbon atoms.
In diamond, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to 4 others, forming a rigid 3D structure. Diamond is the hardest natural material and has a very high melting point. It does not conduct electricity because it has no free electrons.

In graphite, each carbon atom is bonded to 3 others in flat layers of hexagons. Layers in graphite are held by weak forces, so they can slide over each other. Graphite is soft and slippery, making it useful as a lubricant and in pencils. Graphite conducts electricity because it has free electrons in each layer.
The difference in their properties is due to their different bonding arrangements.

A solution is a uniform mixture of a solute dissolved in a solvent. The solute is the substance being dissolved (e.g., sugar, salt). The solvent is the liquid that dissolves the solute (e.g., water, ethanol).
Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a solvent at a given temperature.
Saturated solution contains the maximum solute at that temperature.
Unsaturated solution can still dissolve more solute.
Supersaturated solution contains more solute than normal at that temperature.


Factors affecting solubility include temperature, pressure, and particle size. Solubility is usually expressed in grams of solute per 100g of solvent. Soluble substances dissolve easily; insoluble substances do not.

Filtration removes fine suspended particles using sand and gravel filters.
Chlorination kills harmful microorganisms using chlorine.
Boiling water can also kill germs in small-scale treatment.
Clean water is important to prevent diseases like cholera and typhoid. Treated water is stored in clean tanks before distribution.

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It is made up of tiny, invisible particles called atoms or molecules. The particle theory explains that these particles are in constant motion.

In gases, particles are far apart and move freely in all directions.


Intermolecular forces are strongest in solids and weakest in gases. The movement of particles is affected by temperature. Diffusion is evidence of the particle nature of matter. Brownian motion also shows particles are always moving.

A chemical formula shows the elements in a compound and the ratio of atoms.
Each element is represented by its chemical symbol (e.g., H for Hydrogen, O for Oxygen).
Subscripts in a formula indicate the number of atoms of each element. No subscript means only one atom of that element is present.
Ionic compounds are formed by the combination of positive (cation) and negative (anion) ions.
The charges of ions must balance to make a neutral compound. Metals usually form positive ions, and non-metals form negative ions. The valency of an element shows its combining power and helps in writing formulas.

Chemical compounds are named based on the elements they contain.
The name of an ionic compound starts with the metal (positive ion) followed by the non-metal (negative ion). For simple non-metals, the ending is changed to -ide (e.g., sodium chloride).
If the negative ion contains oxygen, the ending is often -ate (e.g., calcium sulphate) or -ite for less oxygen (e.g., sodium nitrite).
