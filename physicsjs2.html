Work is said to be done when a force moves an object through a distance.
The formula for work is: Work = Force × Distance.
The SI unit of work is Joule (J).
If no movement occurs, no work is done.
Force is measured in Newtons (N), and distance in metres (m)
Work is a scalar quantity, meaning it has magnitude but no direction.
Positive work is done when the force and motion are in the same direction.
Negative work is done when force and motion are in opposite directions.
Zero work occurs when there's no displacement.
Example: If a force of 10 N moves an object 5 m, work = 10 × 5 = 50 J.

Energy is the ability to do work.
There are different forms of energy — kinetic and potential being key mechanical forms.
Kinetic Energy (K.E) is the energy of a moving body: K.E = ½mv².
Potential Energy (P.E) is stored energy due to position: P.E = mgh.
m = mass (kg), v = velocity (m/s), g = 10 m/s², h = height (m).
The unit for both K.E and P.E is the Joule (J).
The faster an object moves, the greater its kinetic energy.
The higher an object is lifted, the greater its potential energy.
Energy can be converted between kinetic and potential forms.
Example: A 2 kg object moving at 3 m/s has K.E = ½ × 2 × 3² = 9 J.

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is used.
The formula is: Power = Work ÷ Time.
The SI unit of power is the Watt (W).
1 Watt = 1 Joule per second.
A larger unit is the kilowatt (kW): 1 kW = 1000 W.
The faster the work is done, the greater the power.
Power is also a scalar quantity.
Power can be used to compare machines or human effort.
Example: If 100 J of work is done in 20 seconds, Power = 100 ÷ 20 = 5 W.
Machines with higher power ratings do work faster.

Kinetic theory explains the behavior of matter in terms of particles in motion.
It is used to describe solids, liquids, and gases.
All matter is made up of tiny particles (atoms or molecules).
These particles are in constant random motion.
The temperature of a substance is a measure of the average kinetic energy of its particles.
Collisions between particles are perfectly elastic (no loss of energy).
Particles exert no forces on each other except during collisions.
The volume of individual particles is negligible compared to the space between them.
The pressure of a gas results from collisions of particles with container walls.
The theory is more accurate for ideal gases under low pressure and high temperature.

The kinetic theory explains how particles behave in solids, liquids, and gases.
In solids, particles vibrate in fixed positions, giving solids a definite shape.
In liquids, particles are close but can slide over each other, allowing flow.
In gases, particles move freely and at high speed in all directions.
Heat energy increases the speed of particle movement.
Expansion happens when particles gain energy and move apart.
Compression occurs when particles are forced closer together.
Diffusion (mixing of particles) happens faster in gases due to high particle speed.
The smell of perfume spreading is due to diffusion.
Boiling and evaporation are explained by increased particle motion due to heat.

Boiling is the rapid vaporization of a liquid at a specific temperature (boiling point).
It happens throughout the liquid, not just at the surface.
Evaporation is the slow change of liquid to gas at the surface, below boiling point.
Both involve the movement of fast-moving particles escaping into the air.
Boiling occurs at a constant temperature, but evaporation can happen at any temperature.
Heat energy is required for both processes.
Wind and surface area increase the rate of evaporation.
Evaporation causes cooling because particles with higher energy leave.
Sweat drying on the body is an example of evaporation.
Boiling water on fire is an example of boiling.

Temperature is the measure of how hot or cold something is.
It is measured using an instrument called a thermometer.
The common temperature units are degree Celsius (°C) and Fahrenheit (°F).
The clinical thermometer is used to measure body temperature.
The laboratory thermometer measures temperatures in experiments.
Thermometers contain liquids like mercury or alcohol that expand with heat.
Temperature affects the state and behavior of matter.
Body temperature is about 37°C.
Ice melts at 0°C and water boils at 100°C at sea level.
Proper thermometer use gives accurate temperature readings.

Heat is a form of energy that moves from a hotter object to a cooler one.
The three main methods of heat transfer are conduction, convection, and radiation.
Conduction occurs mainly in solids, where heat moves through direct contact.
Convection happens in liquids and gases as warm particles rise and cool ones sink.
Radiation transfers heat through empty space using electromagnetic waves.
Metals are good conductors of heat, while wood and plastic are poor conductors (insulators).
A conductor allows heat to pass through easily; an insulator does not.
Convection currents are responsible for heating water and air.
Radiation does not need a medium; this is how the sun heats the earth.
Everyday examples include cooking, ironing, and feeling heat from a fire.

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see.
It travels in a straight line and moves very fast.
The natural source of light is the sun.
Other sources include candles, lamps, fire, and electric bulbs.
Light is essential for vision, photosynthesis, and solar energy.
Light does not need a medium to travel — it can move through a vacuum.
Light helps plants make food through photosynthesis.
Shadows are formed when light is blocked by an object.
Transparent, translucent, and opaque materials affect how light passes.
The study of light is called optics.

Transmission of light means how light passes through materials
Transparent materials allow light to pass through completely (e.g., glass).
Translucent materials allow some light to pass through (e.g., frosted glass).
Opaque materials do not allow any light to pass (e.g., wood).
The ability of a material to transmit light affects visibility and shadow formation.
Shadows are formed by opaque objects blocking light.
Transparent objects do not form clear shadows.
Translucent objects form faint shadows.
Opaque objects form dark and sharp shadows.
Understanding light transmission helps in choosing materials for windows, lamps, etc.

Reflection is the bouncing back of light from a surface.
It helps us to see objects that do not produce their own light.
A plane mirror reflects light regularly.
Smooth, shiny surfaces reflect light better than rough ones.
The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.
The incident ray is the incoming light ray, and the reflected ray is the outgoing one.
The point where the rays meet the surface is the point of incidence.
The normal line is perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence.
Regular reflection produces clear images; diffuse reflection does not.
Mirrors, polished metals, and still water are good reflectors.

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another.
It occurs due to a change in the speed of light between different media.
When light travels from air into water or glass, it slows down and bends.
If light enters a denser medium, it bends towards the normal.
If it enters a less dense medium, it bends away from the normal.
The angle of incidence is the angle between the incoming ray and the normal.
The angle of refraction is the angle between the refracted ray and the normal.
A common example of refraction is a pencil appearing bent in water.
Refraction is used in lenses, spectacles, and optical instruments.
The refractive index measures how much light slows down in a medium.

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its component colors.
This happens when white light passes through a prism.
The colors produced are red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet.
These seven colors make up the visible spectrum.
Violet bends the most, while red bends the least.
Each color has a different wavelength and speed in a medium.
The bending of light in a prism causes the colors to separate.
Dispersion proves that white light is made up of many colors.
It is seen in rainbows and prism experiments.
Dispersion is used in spectroscopy and optical instruments.

Additive mixing is the process of creating new colors by combining light colors.
The primary colors of light are red, green, and blue (RGB).
When red and green light mix, they produce yellow.
Blue and green light produce cyan.
Red and blue light produce magenta.
When all three primary light colors combine, white light is formed.
Additive mixing is different from mixing pigments or paints.
It is used in television screens, computer monitors, and stage lighting.
Secondary colors are formed by combining two primary colors.
Additive color mixing deals with light, not paints or dyes.

Sound is a form of energy produced by vibrating objects.
Vibration is the back-and-forth movement of an object.
All sounds start with a source that vibrates.
These vibrations cause particles in the air to move, creating sound waves.
Sound waves travel through solids, liquids, and gases but not in a vacuum.
Faster vibrations produce higher-pitched sounds.
Slower vibrations produce lower-pitched sounds.
Louder sounds have stronger vibrations (greater amplitude).
The human ear detects vibrations and converts them to signals the brain can understand.
Musical instruments produce sound through different types of vibrations.

The main properties of sound are pitch, loudness, and quality (timbre).
Pitch is how high or low a sound is, depending on the frequency.
Loudness is how strong or soft a sound is, related to amplitude.
Quality helps us distinguish different sounds even if they have the same pitch.
Sound travels faster in solids, slower in gases.
The speed of sound in air is about 343 m/s at room temperature.
Echoes occur due to the reflection of sound.
The human ear can hear sounds from about 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.
Sounds below 20 Hz are called infrasonic, and above 20,000 Hz are ultrasonic.
Sound intensity is measured in decibels (dB).

 Sound can be reflected, just like light.
Reflection of sound occurs when sound waves bounce off a surface.
A hard, smooth surface reflects sound better than a soft surface.
An echo is a reflected sound heard after a delay.
For an echo to be heard, the sound must travel a distance of at least 17 meters.
Echoes are used in sonar and ultrasound scanning.
Reverberation occurs when reflected sounds overlap and make the sound unclear.
Soft materials like curtains and carpets absorb sound.
The time interval between the original sound and the echo helps measure distance.
Bats and dolphins use echolocation to navigate.

Magnetism is the force by which materials attract or repel each other.
A magnet attracts objects made of iron, nickel, or cobalt.
Magnets have two poles: North pole and South pole.
Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.
Magnetic force is strongest at the poles of a magnet.
Magnetic field is the space around a magnet where its force is felt.
A compass uses a magnetized needle to show direction.
Temporary magnets lose their magnetism quickly; permanent magnets retain it.
Electromagnets are magnets made using electricity.
Magnets are used in motors, generators, speakers, and compasses.

A magnetic field is the invisible area around a magnet where magnetic forces can be felt.
Magnetic fields are strongest at the poles of a magnet.
The direction of a magnetic field is from the North Pole to the South Pole.
Iron filings can be used to show the pattern of magnetic field lines.
A compass needle aligns itself with Earth's magnetic field.
Magnetic field lines never cross each other.
Like poles of magnets repel each other; unlike poles attract.
Earth behaves like a giant magnet due to its molten iron core.
Magnetic materials include iron, cobalt, and nickel.
Magnetic fields are important in electricity generation and electric motors.

An electromagnet is a temporary magnet made by passing electric current through a coil of wire.
The strength of an electromagnet increases with more coils or higher current.
A soft iron core is often placed inside the coil to enhance magnetism.
Electromagnets can be turned on or off, unlike permanent magnets.
They are widely used in electric bells, motors, and cranes.
Wrapping the coil around a nail makes a simple electromagnet.
The magnetic field around an electromagnet disappears when the current stops.
Electromagnets are safer and more flexible than permanent magnets.
Electromagnetism combines electricity and magnetism.
The strength of the field depends on the number of wire turns and current.

An electric circuit is a path through which electric current flows.

Wires connect all components and allow current flow.
A switch is used to open or close the circuit.
A bulb lights up when current flows through it.
Circuits can be series or parallel.
In a series circuit, components share the same path.
In a parallel circuit, components have separate paths.
