Chemicals are substances used in laboratories, industries, and homes for various purposes.
They can be classified based on their risk to health and the environment.
Hazardous chemicals can cause serious harm, such as acids, alkalis, and poisons.
Moderately hazardous chemicals are harmful but not as dangerous, e.g., bleach or cleaning agents.
Non-hazardous chemicals pose little or no harm, such as salt and sugar.
Chemical symbols are short representations of elements, e.g., H for Hydrogen, O for Oxygen.
Symbols are derived from Latin or English names of elements.
Safety signs are used to show the level of danger a chemical poses.
Proper labeling of chemicals is essential for safe handling and storage.
Always read the chemical label and symbol before use.

Chemical safety refers to measures taken to avoid accidents and injuries.
Always wear safety gear like gloves, goggles, and lab coats when handling chemicals.
Chemicals should be clearly labeled and stored in their proper containers.
Never taste or directly inhale chemicals.
Work in well-ventilated areas or use fume cupboards.
Know the meaning of safety signs and follow lab safety rules.
Wash hands after handling any chemical.
Dispose of chemicals properly, not in the sink unless instructed.
Keep fire extinguishers and first aid kits close when working with chemicals.
Report any chemical spill or injury immediately to the teacher.

Crude oil is a thick, dark liquid found underground, formed from decayed plants and animals.
It is a major natural resource found in Nigeria, especially in the Niger Delta region.
Crude oil is a mixture of hydrocarbons.
It is extracted from the ground using drilling machines.
The process of turning crude oil into useful products is called refining.
A refinery is where crude oil is separated into different components by fractional distillation.
Products obtained include petrol, diesel, kerosene, and gas.
Crude oil is non-renewable and must be used wisely.
It plays a major role in Nigeria's economy through export and revenue.
Pollution from crude oil (oil spills) harms the environment.

An element is a pure substance made up of only one type of atom.
There are over 100 known elements, each with its own unique properties.
Elements can be metals, non-metals, or metalloids.
A compound is formed when two or more elements chemically combine.
Compounds have properties different from the elements that form them.
Compounds are represented by chemical formulas (e.g., H₂O).
The smallest part of a compound is a molecule or a formula unit.
Elements cannot be broken down into simpler substances chemically.
Compounds can be broken down into elements using chemical reactions.
Mixtures are different from compounds because their substances are not chemically combined.

Every element is represented by a unique chemical symbol (e.g., H for hydrogen).
Chemical symbols are usually one or two letters long.
The first letter is always capitalized; the second, if present, is lowercase.
Chemical formulae show the types and numbers of atoms in a compound.
H₂O means two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom.
The subscript number indicates the number of atoms of that element.
No subscript means only one atom of that element.
Compounds always have fixed ratios of elements.
The Periodic Table lists all chemical symbols.
Correct use of symbols and formulae is essential in writing chemical equations.

A binary compound is made up of two elements chemically combined.
An example of a binary compound is NaCl (sodium chloride).
Binary compounds often end with the suffix “-ide” (e.g., oxide, sulfide).
A ternary compound contains three different elements.
Ternary compounds often include polyatomic ions (e.g., nitrate NO₃⁻).
An example of a ternary compound is CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate).
Ternary compounds may end in “-ate” or “-ite”.
Binary compounds are usually simpler than ternary compounds.
Proper naming depends on understanding the valency of elements.
Both binary and ternary compounds have fixed formulas and unique properties.

A chemical change results in the formation of new substances.
During a chemical change, the chemical composition of the substance is altered.
Chemical changes are often irreversible.
They involve breaking and forming of chemical bonds.
Examples include rusting, burning, and cooking.
Chemical changes often release or absorb energy (heat, light, sound).
The original substances are known as reactants.
The new substances formed are called products.
Chemical changes differ from physical changes (which are reversible).
Signs such as color change or gas release indicate chemical changes.

Chemical reactions can be classified into different types.
Combination reactions involve two or more substances forming one product.
Decomposition reactions involve a single substance breaking into simpler parts.
Displacement reactions involve one element replacing another in a compound.
Double displacement reactions involve exchange of ions between two compounds.
Combustion reactions involve burning in the presence of oxygen.
Neutralization reactions occur between acids and bases to form salt and water.
Each reaction type has a unique pattern or general equation.
Recognizing the type helps predict products.
Real-life examples include digestion (decomposition) and rusting (combination).

A chemical equation represents a chemical reaction using symbols.
The reactants are written on the left; products on the right.
A plus sign separates multiple reactants or products.
An arrow (→) shows the direction of the reaction.
Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O.
Symbols indicate the physical state: (s), (l), (g), (aq).
A word equation uses names instead of symbols.
Chemical equations must be written accurately.
They show what is used and what is produced.
Writing equations helps in understanding and calculating reactions.

Chemical equations must be balanced to follow the law of conservation of mass.
The number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides.
Coefficients are added to balance equations (not subscripts).
Balancing ensures no atoms are lost or created.
Start by balancing elements that appear once on each side.
Leave hydrogen and oxygen for last, if they appear in many compounds.
Practice is key to balancing equations correctly.
Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O becomes 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.
Never change chemical formulas when balancing.
Balanced equations are essential for accurate chemical calculations.

Acids are substances that release hydrogen ions (H⁺) in water.
Bases are substances that release hydroxide ions (OH⁻) or accept H⁺ ions.
When an acid reacts with a base, a neutralization reaction occurs.
The products of neutralization are salt and water.
A common example is: Hydrochloric acid + Sodium hydroxide → Sodium chloride + Water.
Strong acids and bases fully dissociate in water; weak ones only partially dissociate.
Neutralization is used in making toothpaste, medicine, and treating acidic soil.
The reaction between an acid and a metal produces salt and hydrogen gas.
Acid + Metal carbonate → Salt + Water + Carbon dioxide.
Acid-base reactions are essential in biological and industrial processes.

The pH scale measures how acidic or basic a solution is, ranging from 0 to 14.
A pH of 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, and above 7 is basic (alkaline).
Acids have pH values from 0–6; bases have pH values from 8–14.
Indicators are substances that change color to show whether a substance is an acid or base.
Common indicators include litmus, methyl orange, and phenolphthalein.
Litmus turns red in acid and blue in base.
Methyl orange turns red in acid and yellow in base.
Phenolphthalein is colorless in acid and pink in base.
Universal indicator shows a range of colors to indicate specific pH values.
pH indicators are useful in agriculture, medicine, and lab experiments.

All matter is made up of tiny particles called atoms.
An atom consists of a nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) and electrons.
Protons are positively charged, neutrons have no charge, and electrons are negatively charged.
The atomic number is the number of protons in an atom.
The mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons.
Electrons move in energy levels or shells around the nucleus.
The first shell holds 2 electrons, the second holds up to 8, and so on.
Atoms of the same element have the same number of protons.
Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
Electrons determine the chemical properties of an element.

The periodic table organizes elements based on their atomic number.
Vertical columns are called groups; there are 18 groups.
Horizontal rows are called periods; there are 7 periods.
Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties.
Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells.
Group number tells the number of electrons in the outer shell (for Groups 1–2 and 13–18).
Period number tells the number of occupied energy levels.
Metals are found on the left side, non-metals on the right.
Group 1 contains alkali metals, Group 17 contains halogens, and Group 18 contains noble gases.
Transition metals are found in the center block of the table.

Group trends are related to changes in atomic size and electron configuration.

Chemical bonding is the force that holds atoms or ions together in compounds.
Atoms bond to achieve a full outer shell of electrons (noble gas configuration).
There are three major types of chemical bonds: ionic, covalent, and metallic.
Bonding depends on the type of atoms involved—metals or non-metals.
Ionic bonding involves the transfer of electrons from one atom to another.
Covalent bonding involves the sharing of electrons between atoms.
Metallic bonding involves a ‘sea’ of delocalized electrons around metal ions.
Bonding affects physical and chemical properties like melting point and conductivity.
Electronegativity differences influence the type of bond formed.
Understanding bonding is key to understanding how substances behave.

Ionic bonding occurs between metals and non-metals.
Metals lose electrons to become positively charged ions (cations).
Non-metals gain electrons to become negatively charged ions (anions).\
The oppositely charged ions attract each other strongly.
Ionic compounds form giant crystal lattice structures.
They have high melting and boiling points due to strong electrostatic forces.
Ionic compounds conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water.
Sodium chloride (NaCl) is a common example of an ionic compound.
Electron transfer diagrams show how ionic bonds form.
The number of electrons transferred depends on the valency of the atoms.

Covalent bonding occurs between non-metal atoms.
Atoms share pairs of electrons to achieve a full outer shell.
A single covalent bond shares one pair of electrons.
Double and triple bonds involve two or three shared pairs.
Common covalent molecules include H₂, O₂, CO₂, and H₂O.
Covalent compounds may exist as simple molecules or giant structures.
They generally have low melting and boiling points (if simple molecules).
Most do not conduct electricity, even when molten.
Dot-and-cross diagrams show shared electrons.
Covalent bonding is directional—between specific atoms.

Metallic bonding occurs between metal atoms.
Metal atoms lose outer electrons, forming positive ions.
The electrons become delocalized and move freely.
The attraction between ions and electrons forms a strong bond.
This structure is often described as a lattice of cations in a sea of electrons.
Metallic bonding explains high electrical and thermal conductivity.
Metals are malleable and ductile due to layers of atoms that slide.
The strength of metallic bonds varies with the number of delocalized electrons.
Alloys are mixtures of metals, often with enhanced properties.
Examples of metallic elements include iron, copper, and aluminum.
