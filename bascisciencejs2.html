        All things around us are classified into living and non-living things.
Living things are things that are alive and carry out life processes.
Examples of living things include humans, animals, and plants.
Non-living things do not have life and cannot perform life processes.
Examples of non-living things are stones, books, chairs, and air.
Living things grow, reproduce, respond to stimuli, and need food.
Non-living things do not grow, reproduce, or need food.
Some non-living things are made by man (artificial), e.g. tables.
Others are found in nature (natural), e.g. water and rocks.
Understanding the differences helps us take care of our environment.

        Energy is the ability to do work or cause change.


It exists in different forms: kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical, electrical, and nuclear.


Kinetic energy is energy of motion, while potential energy is stored energy.


Energy can be transferred (moved from one object to another).


It can also be transformed (changed from one form to another).


The unit of energy is the joule (J).


An object in motion possesses kinetic energy proportional to its mass and speed.


Energy stored in food, fuel, or batteries is chemical energy.


Law of conservation of energy states energy cannot be created or destroyed.


Energy is always conserved, but some is often lost as heat during transformation.

        Gravitational force is the attractive force between two masses.


It is calculated using the formula: F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r².


G is the gravitational constant: 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ Nm²/kg².


m₁ and m₂ are the masses of the two objects in kilograms.


r is the distance between the centers of the two masses in meters.


Gravity acts downward towards the center of Earth.


On Earth, weight is the gravitational force acting on an object.


Weight = mass × gravitational field strength (g = 9.8 N/kg).


As distance increases, gravitational force decreases.


The gravitational force is always attractive, never repulsive.
