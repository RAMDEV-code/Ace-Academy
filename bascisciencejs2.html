Organisms in the same habitat often interact with one another.
These interactions can be beneficial, harmful, or neutral.
Symbiosis is a close relationship between two species.
Mutualism is when both organisms benefit (e.g., bee and flower).
Commensalism is when one benefits and the other is unaffected (e.g., barnacles on whales).
Parasitism is when one benefits and the other is harmed (e.g., lice on humans).
Competition occurs when organisms fight for limited resources.
Predation is when one organism hunts and feeds on another.
These relationships help maintain balance in the habitat.
Human activities can disrupt these relationships.

Potential energy is the energy stored in an object due to its position.
It is usually found in objects held at height.
Kinetic energy is the energy possessed by a moving object.
The formula for potential energy is: PE = mgh.
The formula for kinetic energy is: KE = ½mv².
Both forms of energy are measured in joules (J).
The higher or heavier an object, the more potential energy it has.
The faster an object moves, the more kinetic energy it has.
Energy can change from potential to kinetic and vice versa.
Examples: A book on a shelf has potential energy; a moving car has kinetic energy.

Work done (W) is the energy transferred when a force moves an object over a distance: 
W = F × d. (F = force in newtons, d = distance in metres). The unit of work is joule (J). 
Example: If 10 N of force moves an object 5 m, Work = 10 × 5 = 50 J
Power (P) is the rate at which work is done: P = W ÷ t. (W = work in joules, t = time in seconds)  Example: If 100 J of work is done in 20 s, Power = 100 ÷ 20 = 5 W
Potential energy (PE) is stored energy due to height: 
PE = m × g × h (m = mass in kg, g = 10 m/s², h = height in m) 
Example: find the PE of 2 kg object at 10 m = 2 × 10 × 10 = 200 J
Kinetic energy (KE) is the energy of a moving object: KE = ½ × m × v² (v = speed in m/s) Example:find KE of 4 kg object moving at 3 m/s = ½ × 4 × 3² = 18 J
To solve problems, always use consistent units (kg, m, s, N, J, W). Example: Convert minutes to seconds when calculating power.
The greater the height or mass, the more potential energy an object has. The faster an object moves, the greater its kinetic energy.
Energy transfers from one form to another: Potential → Kinetic (e.g., falling ball).

Energy is always transferred when work is done. The object receiving the force gains energy. The source loses the same amount of energy.
For example, lifting a load transfers chemical energy from muscles to kinetic energy.
When a car engine works, fuel's chemical energy is converted to mechanical energy.
Energy transfer is never 100% efficient—some is lost as heat or sound. Machines transfer energy from one part to another.
Energy can change forms: chemical to heat, kinetic to potential, etc.
The Law of Conservation of Energy states energy cannot be created or destroyed. Total energy remains constant, only the form changes.

Heating a substance increases particle energy and motion while Cooling slows down particle motion.
Kinetic theory helps explain changes of state (solid, liquid, gas). It is a key concept in understanding the nature of matter.

The kinetic theory of matter explains that all matter is made up of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are in constant motion. These particles have kinetic energy, which increases with temperature. The theory helps explain many natural phenomena:
Diffusion occurs because particles move from high to low concentration.
Smell of perfume spreads due to gas particle movement.
A drop of ink spreads in water due to liquid particle motion.
Heating a solid causes particles to vibrate faster and expand.
Boiling water forms bubbles due to rapid movement of particles.
Gases fill any container because particles move freely.
Ice melts when particles gain energy and move apart.
Evaporation occurs when surface particles escape into air.
The kinetic theory unifies these observations by linking them to the motion and interactions of particles, making it a powerful model for understanding matter.

Boiling and evaporation are methods of changing liquid to gas. Boiling is the rapid vaporization of a liquid that occurs when its vapour pressure equals the external pressure. It happens throughout the liquid, not just at the surface. 
Evaporation is the process by which molecules at the surface of a liquid escape into the gas phase at temperatures below the boiling point
Boiling happens at a fixed temperature called boiling point while evaporation occurs at any temperature from the surface.
Boiling is a faster process than evaporation. Wind, heat, and surface area affect the rate of evaporation. Boiling requires a heat source, evaporation does not.
Both processes involve the gain of energy by particles. Evaporation is useful in drying clothes and cooling the body.

Thermal energy is the energy that comes from heat. Heat flows from a hotter object to a cooler one. The flow of heat continues until both objects reach the same temperature. This flow of heat is called heat transfer. The direction of heat flow is always from high temperature to low temperature.
Materials that allow heat to flow easily are conductors (e.g., metals). Materials that do not allow heat to flow easily are insulators (e.g., wood, plastic).
A thermometer is used to measure temperature, not heat. The SI unit of temperature is Kelvin (K), but Celsius (°C) is commonly used. Thermal equilibrium occurs when there is no net flow of heat between objects.

Heat transfer is the movement of thermal energy from a region of higher temperature to one of lower temperature. There are three main methods: conduction, convection, and radiation.
Conduction occurs mainly in solids when heat passes through a material without the particles moving from place to place. Metals are good conductors because of their free electrons, while wood and plastic are poor conductors (insulators).
Convection is the transfer of heat in fluids (liquids and gases) by the movement of the fluid itself. Heated fluid becomes less dense and rises, while cooler fluid sinks, forming convection currents.
Radiation is the transfer of heat energy in the form of electromagnetic waves, without the need for a medium. All objects emit and absorb radiant energy; black, dull surfaces are good absorbers and emitters, while shiny surfaces are poor absorbers.
Heat transfer plays important roles in cooking, engineering, climate, and daily life. Controlling heat transfer is essential in insulation, refrigeration, and energy efficiency.

Reproductive health is the state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being in all matters concerning the reproductive system. It goes beyond the absence of disease to include safe sexual practices, personal hygiene, and healthy reproductive development.
Maintaining good reproductive health helps prevent diseases, unwanted pregnancies, and complications that may affect future well-being. One major threat to reproductive health is sexually transmitted infections (STIs) such as HIV/AIDS, gonorrhea, and syphilis.
Personal hygiene is especially important during puberty when the body undergoes physical and hormonal changes. Poor hygiene can increase the risk of infections.
Early pregnancy can negatively affect a teenager’s education, future opportunities, and overall health. Both boys and girls must take responsibility for protecting their reproductive health.
Reproductive health education equips young people with accurate information, enabling them to make informed and responsible decisions about their bodies. It also encourages respect for oneself and others.
By practicing safe habits, seeking medical help when necessary, and staying informed, individuals can maintain a healthy reproductive life and contribute to a healthier society.

Chemicals are substances with definite composition, made of elements or compounds, and can be natural or man-made. They are essential in everyday life, playing vital roles in health, industry, and the environment.
Chemicals are classified based on their uses into different categories:
Domestic chemicals – used in homes for cleaning, cooking, and preservation (e.g., detergents, baking soda, vinegar).
Agricultural chemicals – used to improve crop yield and protect plants (e.g., fertilizers, pesticides, herbicides).
Industrial chemicals – used in manufacturing processes (e.g., sulphuric acid, caustic soda, ammonia).
Medical chemicals – used for treatment and prevention of diseases (e.g., aspirin, antiseptics, vaccines).
Food chemicals – used to enhance flavour, preserve food, or improve nutrition (e.g., salt, citric acid, food colouring).
Laboratory chemicals – used in experiments and research (e.g., ethanol, acids, indicators).
While chemicals are beneficial, misuse can cause environmental pollution, health hazards, and accidents, hence the need for proper handling, storage, and disposal.

Chemicals can be hazardous, non-hazardous, or moderately hazardous.
Hazardous chemicals can cause harm if misused (e.g., acids, bleach).
Non-hazardous chemicals pose little or no risk (e.g., table salt).
Moderately hazardous chemicals may cause minor harm if not handled well.
Chemical safety includes knowing how to store and use chemicals properly. Always wear gloves, goggles, or masks when handling dangerous chemicals.
Chemical containers must carry hazard symbols that visually warn of the risks. Examples include:
Skull and crossbones (toxic)
Flame (flammable)
Corrosion symbol (corrosive)
Exclamation mark (irritant/harmful)
Dead fish/tree (environmental hazard)
Safety rules require reading labels before use, avoiding direct contact or inhalation, proper waste disposal, and immediate reporting of spills or accidents.

Drugs are substances taken into the body to prevent, treat, or cure diseases. They may be natural (from plants and animals) or synthetic (made in laboratories). When used correctly and according to medical advice, drugs are beneficial to health.
Drugs can be classified into legal drugs (such as paracetamol, antibiotics, and vaccines) and illegal drugs (such as cocaine, heroin, and cannabis) which are prohibited by law. Legal drugs are often regulated and dispensed by pharmacists, while illegal drugs are dangerous and unapproved for medical use.
Drug abuse occurs when a drug is taken without a doctor’s prescription, in excessive amounts, or for purposes other than treatment. It can happen with both legal and illegal drugs. For example, taking antibiotics without medical advice or abusing cough syrup for intoxication are forms of drug abuse.
Common reasons for drug abuse include peer pressure, curiosity, stress relief, and ignorance of harmful effects. Early education about drugs helps young people make wise choices and avoid addiction.
Effects of drug abuse may include damage to vital organs, mental disorders, accidents, violence, and poor academic performance. The first step to prevention is knowledge, self-control, and avoidance of bad company.

Drug addiction is a condition where a person becomes physically or psychologically dependent on a drug. Over time, the body may require higher doses to achieve the same effect, leading to tolerance and severe health problems.
Commonly abused drugs in Nigeria include cannabis, cocaine, heroin, tramadol, codeine syrup, alcohol, and nicotine. Each of these has dangerous effects on the brain, heart, liver, and overall mental stability.
Short-term effects of drug abuse may include dizziness, confusion, poor judgment, and risky behaviour. Long-term effects include liver damage, lung diseases, kidney failure, depression, and even death.
Drug abuse can also lead to social problems like family breakdown, crime, unemployment, and poverty. Addicts may spend all their resources on drugs, neglecting basic needs and responsibilities.
Preventive measures include strict law enforcement, public awareness campaigns, youth empowerment programs, and rehabilitation centres for addicts. Parents and teachers play a crucial role in early detection and intervention.
Rehabilitation involves medical treatment, counselling, and skill training to help addicts recover and reintegrate into society. Avoiding drug abuse is far easier than overcoming addiction.

Crude oil, also called petroleum, is a naturally occurring dark, viscous liquid found beneath the earth’s surface. It is formed from the remains of tiny plants and animals that lived in ancient seas millions of years ago. These remains were buried under layers of sediment and rock, where heat and pressure gradually converted them into crude oil and natural gas.
Crude oil is obtained through drilling deep wells into oil-rich areas called oil fields. The crude oil is then pumped to the surface and transported to refineries for processing. At the refinery, crude oil is separated into different useful products by a process called fractional distillation.
The main products from crude oil include petrol (gasoline), diesel, kerosene, lubricating oil, bitumen, and liquefied petroleum gas (LPG). These products are used for fuel, transportation, road construction, cooking, and as raw materials for making plastics and chemicals.
Nigeria is one of the largest producers of crude oil in Africa, with major oil fields located in the Niger Delta region. Crude oil exports are a major source of revenue for the country. However, oil exploration and usage can lead to environmental problems such as oil spills, air pollution, and gas flaring.
