Genetics is the study of heredity and variation in living organisms. Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring. Variation refers to differences among individuals of the same species.
Gregor Mendel, the father of genetics, used pea plants to establish laws of inheritance. He discovered that traits are controlled by discrete units called genes. Genes exist in pairs, and each parent contributes one gene to offspring.
Mendel’s first law (Law of Segregation) states that alleles of a gene separate during gamete formation. His second law (Law of Independent Assortment) states that alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation.

Genes are located on chromosomes in the nucleus. Each gene controls the expression of a particular trait, such as eye colour or blood group.
Human beings have 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs. One pair determines sex (XX in females and XY in males). Other 22 pairs are autosomes controlling body traits.
Blood groups in humans are an example of multiple alleles (A, B, O) and codominance (AB). Some traits are sex-linked, such as colour blindness and haemophilia, which are more common in males.
Mutation is a sudden change in genes or chromosomes, leading to variation. It may be harmful, beneficial, or neutral.

Bush burning is the uncontrolled setting of vegetation on fire, often for farming or hunting. It destroys soil organic matter, reduces soil fertility, and increases carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas. Frequent bush burning leads to erosion and loss of biodiversity.
Deforestation is the large-scale cutting down of trees without replanting. It reduces forest cover, leading to desert encroachment, loss of wildlife habitats, and reduced rainfall. Trees absorb carbon dioxide and release oxygen; without them, the atmosphere’s balance is disrupted.
Both practices increase greenhouse gases, contributing to climate change. Sustainable practices such as afforestation, controlled grazing, and environmental education can reduce these hazards.

Desertification is the gradual transformation of fertile land into desert, mainly due to overgrazing, deforestation, poor farming methods, and climate change. It reduces soil productivity and leads to food shortages.
Chemically, desert soils lose organic matter and essential nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. This results in low fertility and poor water retention. Desertification also increases carbon dioxide levels as vegetation cover declines.
Human activities such as continuous farming without soil enrichment accelerate the process. Control measures include afforestation, irrigation, controlled grazing, and use of fertilizers to restore soil nutrients.

The ozone layer is a region of the stratosphere rich in ozone (O₃) molecules that absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun. Ozone depletion is the thinning of this layer, mainly caused by chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), halons, and nitrogen oxides.
When CFCs rise to the stratosphere, UV light breaks them down, releasing chlorine atoms which react with ozone, reducing its concentration. One chlorine atom can destroy thousands of ozone molecules.
Effects include increased UV radiation reaching Earth, causing skin cancer, eye cataracts, reduced crop yield, and harm to aquatic life. Global measures like the Montreal Protocol aim to phase out ozone-depleting substances.

The human respiratory system ensures continuous supply of oxygen and removal of carbon dioxide.
 It consists mainly of the nose, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and lungs.
 Air enters through the nose where it is filtered, warmed, and moistened.
 The trachea is a tube supported by cartilage rings to prevent collapse during breathing.
 It divides into bronchi, each leading to a lung.
 The bronchi further divide into bronchioles that end in tiny air sacs called alveoli.
 Alveoli provide a large surface area for gaseous exchange.
 Breathing involves two processes: inspiration (inhaling) and expiration (exhaling).
 During inspiration, the diaphragm contracts and moves downward, while ribs move upward and outward.
 This increases chest volume and decreases pressure, allowing air to enter the lungs.
 During expiration, the diaphragm relaxes and moves upward, while ribs move downward and inward.
 This decreases chest volume and increases pressure, forcing air out of the lungs.
 The respiratory system is closely linked with the circulatory system for efficient oxygen transport.

Gaseous exchange is the process by which oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide is removed.
 In humans, it occurs in the alveoli of the lungs.
 Oxygen diffuses from alveoli into the blood because its concentration is higher in alveoli than in blood.
 At the same time, carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into alveoli due to concentration differences.
 This process is aided by the thin walls of alveoli and their rich capillary network.
 The large number of alveoli provides an extensive surface area for exchange.
 Moist lining of alveoli helps dissolve gases for easy diffusion.
 The blood transports oxygen bound to hemoglobin in red blood cells.
 Carbon dioxide is carried in plasma mainly as bicarbonate ions.
 In plants, gaseous exchange occurs through stomata and lenticels.
 Oxygen is used for respiration, while carbon dioxide is released as waste.
 At night, plants take in oxygen and release carbon dioxide.
 Efficient gaseous exchange is vital for maintaining life processes in both plants and animals.

Science is the systematic study of nature through observation and experimentation.
 The scientific process begins with identifying a problem or asking a question.
 Next is making observations and gathering background information.
 A hypothesis is proposed as a possible explanation.
 An experiment is designed to test the hypothesis under controlled conditions.
 Data collected are recorded in tables and charts for clarity.
 Analysis of data helps determine patterns, trends, or relationships.
 A conclusion is drawn to confirm or reject the hypothesis.
 If results agree, the hypothesis may become a theory.
 If results disagree, the hypothesis is modified and retested.
 Scientific experiments must be repeatable and verifiable.
 The process encourages accuracy, objectivity, and curiosity.
 It is the foundation of discoveries in chemistry and other sciences.

Diagrams are essential tools for representing biological structures.
 They must be neat, clear, and drawn in pencil, not ink.
 Labels should be written in lowercase letters (except proper nouns).
 Label lines must be straight, horizontal, and not crossing each other.
 Diagrams should be large enough to occupy at least two-thirds of the page.
 Shading is discouraged; instead, use simple clear outlines.
 Title of the diagram should be written below, in capital letters.
 Avoid unnecessary details; draw only what is required in the question.
 Proportions must be accurate to show relative sizes of parts.
 Arrows should not be used for labeling, only straight lines.
 Labels must be placed on the right-hand side of the diagram where possible.
 The use of rulers for lines ensures neatness and accuracy.
 Good diagrams improve communication and earn higher marks in examinations.

Living organisms are classified to make study easier and to show relationships among them. The five-kingdom system proposed by Whittaker includes Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia.
The Monera are single-celled organisms without a true nucleus (prokaryotes), such as bacteria. Protista are mostly unicellular eukaryotes like Amoeba and Euglena.
Fungi are multicellular or unicellular organisms without chlorophyll. They absorb food from decaying materials. Plantae are multicellular, autotrophic organisms with chlorophyll that produce their own food through photosynthesis.
Animalia are multicellular heterotrophic organisms that depend on other organisms for food. This kingdom is further divided into invertebrates and vertebrates. Classification helps biologists to organize life forms and understand their evolutionary links.

Binomial nomenclature is the scientific system of naming organisms introduced by Carolus Linnaeus. Each organism is given a two-part Latin name: the genus and the species.
The genus name starts with a capital letter, while the species name begins with a small letter. Both are written in italics when typed or underlined when handwritten, e.g., Homo sapiens.
This system avoids confusion from local names and ensures a universal method of communication. It also shows evolutionary relationships among species.
Scientific names are used worldwide, making it easier for scientists in different countries to identify organisms. This universal language of classification is a foundation for biological studies.

The plant kingdom is classified into four major divisions: Thallophyta, Bryophyta, Pteridophyta, and Spermatophyta.
Thallophyta includes algae with simple, non-vascular structures. Bryophyta are mosses and liverworts that grow in moist areas and reproduce by spores.
Pteridophyta are ferns, vascular but non-seed plants, also reproducing by spores. Spermatophyta are seed-bearing plants further divided into Gymnosperms (naked seeds) and Angiosperms (seeds enclosed in fruits).
Plants are vital because they produce oxygen, provide food, shelter, and raw materials. Their classification is based on presence of vascular tissues, seeds, and flowers.

Invertebrates are animals without backbones. They make up about 95% of all animal species. They are classified into several phyla such as Porifera, Coelenterata, Platyhelminthes, Nematoda, Annelida, Arthropoda, Mollusca, and Echinodermata.
Porifera are sponges with porous bodies. Coelenterata include Hydra and jellyfish. Platyhelminthes are flatworms, while Nematoda are roundworms. Annelida are segmented worms like earthworms.
Arthropoda is the largest phylum, including insects, arachnids, and crustaceans. Mollusca include snails and octopuses, while Echinodermata are marine animals like starfish.
Invertebrates play ecological roles as decomposers, pollinators, and food sources. They also have economic importance and health implications.

Vertebrates are animals with a backbone and internal skeleton. They belong to the phylum Chordata and are divided into five major classes: Pisces, Amphibia, Reptilia, Aves, and Mammalia.
Pisces are fish adapted to aquatic life with gills and fins. Amphibia live both on land and in water, e.g., frogs. Reptilia are cold-blooded animals with scales, e.g., lizards and snakes.
Aves are birds with feathers, wings, and beaks. They are warm-blooded and lay eggs. Mammalia are warm-blooded animals with mammary glands, fur or hair, and give birth to young ones.
Vertebrates are advanced animals with developed organ systems and high mobility. They are important in ecological balance and human survival.
