The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life. Robert Hooke first described the cell in 1665 using a light microscope, while Schleiden and Schwann later proposed the Cell Theory, which states that all living things are made up of cells, and cells are the unit of structure and function. Virchow added that all cells arise from pre-existing cells.
Cells contain organelles that perform specific functions. The nucleus controls activities, mitochondria produce energy, ribosomes synthesize proteins, and lysosomes digest wastes. Endoplasmic reticulum transports materials, while Golgi apparatus modifies and packages substances. Plant cells have chloroplasts for photosynthesis, cell walls for rigidity, and large vacuoles for storage. Animal cells lack a cell wall and chloroplast but have centrioles for cell division.
Thus, both plant and animal cells share common structures but differ in organelles suited to their functions.

Cells exist in different forms to adapt to survival. Some organisms are unicellular, e.g., Amoeba and Paramecium. These perform all life processes in one cell. Others exist in colonies, e.g., Volvox, where many similar cells cluster but can still survive independently.
Filamentous forms are seen in algae like Spirogyra, where identical cells join end-to-end forming long threads. In multicellular organisms like plants and animals, cells are highly specialized and exist as part of tissues, organs, and systems.
This diversity in cell form allows organisms to function efficiently in different environments, from simple unicellular life to complex multicellular structures.

Living organisms exhibit hierarchical organization. The cell is the basic unit of life. Similar cells performing the same function form a tissue (e.g., muscle tissue, xylem). Different tissues combine to form an organ (e.g., heart, leaf). A group of organs working together makes up an organ system (e.g., circulatory system, digestive system).
Cell →Tissue →Organ → System
In multicellular organisms, several organ systems integrate to form the entire organism, ensuring efficient survival. This structural arrangement allows specialization, division of labour, and increased complexity in higher organisms compared to unicellular life.

Cell differentiation is the process by which unspecialized cells develop into specialized cells with distinct structures and functions. In animals, stem cells differentiate into muscle, nerve, or blood cells. In plants, meristematic cells differentiate into xylem, phloem, or guard cells.
Specialization allows cells to perform unique roles. For example, red blood cells transport oxygen, nerve cells transmit impulses, and palisade cells carry out photosynthesis. Differentiation ensures division of labour, efficiency, and adaptability of organisms.
Failure of differentiation may result in abnormal growth or malfunction of tissues. Thus, it is vital for normal growth, repair, and development of living organisms.

Cellular respiration is the process by which living organisms release energy from food substances, mainly glucose, to power cellular activities. It occurs in all living cells. Energy is stored in Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP), the universal energy currency.
There are two main types: aerobic and anaerobic respiration. Aerobic respiration occurs in the presence of oxygen and produces a large amount of energy. Anaerobic respiration occurs in the absence of oxygen and yields less energy.
The general equation of aerobic respiration is:
 Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy (ATP)
 C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP
Aerobic respiration takes place mainly in the mitochondria, known as the “powerhouse of the cell.” It involves three stages: glycolysis (in the cytoplasm), Krebs cycle (in the mitochondrial matrix), and electron transport chain (in the inner mitochondrial membrane).
This process is highly efficient, releasing about 38 molecules of ATP from one glucose molecule. The released ATP powers processes such as active transport, muscle contraction, biosynthesis, and nerve transmission.

Anaerobic respiration occurs when glucose is broken down in the absence of oxygen. It is less efficient than aerobic respiration because it releases only a small amount of energy.
In animals (muscle cells):
 Glucose → Lactic acid + Energy (2 ATP)
In yeast and some microorganisms (alcoholic fermentation):
 Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Energy (2 ATP)
Anaerobic respiration occurs during strenuous exercise in humans when oxygen supply is insufficient, leading to the accumulation of lactic acid, which causes muscle fatigue and cramps.
In yeast, anaerobic respiration (fermentation) is used in brewing, wine-making, and bread production. Carbon dioxide released helps dough rise, while ethanol forms alcohol.
Though less efficient, anaerobic respiration is important in energy production under oxygen-limited conditions and has wide industrial applications. The process is reversible in animals when oxygen becomes available again (oxygen debt repayment).

Growth is a permanent and irreversible increase in size, mass, and complexity of an organism due to cell division, enlargement, and differentiation. It involves both anabolic (building) and catabolic (breaking down) processes, but anabolism must exceed catabolism for growth to occur.
In plants, growth is mainly restricted to meristematic tissues (apical meristem at tips of roots and shoots). Growth can be measured using parameters such as increase in height, volume, fresh or dry mass, and leaf area.
Animals show uniform growth distributed all over the body, while plants exhibit localized growth. External factors like light, water, nutrients, and temperature influence growth, while internal factors include hormones like auxins, gibberellins, and cytokinins.

Irritability is the ability of organisms to perceive and respond to changes (stimuli) in their environment. This property enables organisms to survive by adapting to favorable and unfavorable conditions. Stimuli can be internal (hunger, hormones) or external (light, temperature, touch, sound).
Plants show tropic responses such as phototropism, geotropism, hydrotropism, and thigmotropism, usually regulated by hormones like auxins. Animals show quicker responses through their nervous and endocrine systems.
At the cellular level, receptors detect stimuli and initiate changes such as movement of ions, production of hormones, or changes in enzyme activity. Reflex actions are rapid automatic responses in animals that help in protection and survival.

Reproduction is the biological process by which organisms give rise to new individuals of the same species. It ensures continuity of life. There are two main types: asexual and sexual reproduction.
Asexual reproduction involves a single parent and produces offspring that are genetically identical (clones). Methods include binary fission, budding, spore formation, vegetative propagation, and regeneration.
Sexual reproduction involves two parents, formation of gametes, fertilization, and genetic variation in offspring. It occurs in both plants (flowers and pollination) and animals (gametes and fertilization). Sexual reproduction increases adaptability through variation, while asexual reproduction is faster and requires less energy.

Asexual reproduction is the process by which organisms produce new individuals from a single parent without the fusion of gametes. The offspring are genetically identical to the parent and to one another, making them clones.
It is common in lower organisms such as bacteria, fungi, protozoa, algae, and some plants. Higher plants also reproduce asexually through structures like bulbs, tubers, rhizomes, and runners. Animals such as Hydra, sponges, and starfish also show asexual reproduction.
Methods of asexual reproduction include binary fission, budding, spore formation, vegetative propagation, and fragmentation. Binary fission occurs in unicellular organisms such as Amoeba and Paramecium, while budding occurs in yeast and Hydra.
Spore formation is common in fungi like Rhizopus, which produces numerous spores that germinate under favorable conditions. Vegetative propagation in plants can be natural (e.g., bulbs of onion, tubers of yam, rhizomes of ginger) or artificial (cuttings, grafting, layering, tissue culture).
Advantages of asexual reproduction include rapid population increase, less energy and time consumption, and the ability to reproduce without a mate. However, its major disadvantage is the lack of genetic variation, which makes organisms vulnerable to diseases and environmental changes.

Excretion is the biological process by which organisms remove metabolic wastes from their bodies to maintain homeostasis. It is different from egestion, which is the removal of undigested food. In invertebrates, various structures perform the excretory role depending on the level of organization.
In protozoa such as Amoeba, excretion occurs by simple diffusion through the cell membrane because of their large surface area to volume ratio. In flatworms (Platyhelminthes), specialized flame cells with cilia remove waste materials through tubules. Earthworms (Annelids) use nephridia, long coiled tubules that filter body fluid and excrete nitrogenous wastes.
In insects, the excretory organs are Malpighian tubules. These are long, thin tubules that extend from the gut and absorb nitrogenous wastes, converting them mainly into uric acid to conserve water.
Excretory products vary among invertebrates. Ammonia is common in aquatic invertebrates (ammonotelic), urea in some land organisms (ureotelic), and uric acid in insects and land snails (uricotelic). Thus, excretory systems reflect both the complexity of the animal and its habitat.

In humans, excretion involves the removal of metabolic wastes such as carbon dioxide, water, salts, and nitrogenous compounds. The main excretory organs are the lungs (carbon dioxide and water vapour), skin (sweat – water, salts, and urea), liver (bile pigments from breakdown of red blood cells), and kidneys (urea, water, and salts).
The kidneys are the principal organs of excretion. Each kidney contains about a million nephrons, the functional units where urine production occurs. Urine formation takes place in three main stages: ultrafiltration, selective reabsorption, and tubular secretion.
During ultrafiltration, blood is filtered under high pressure in the Bowman’s capsule, removing water, salts, glucose, amino acids, and urea. In selective reabsorption, essential substances such as glucose, amino acids, and some salts are reabsorbed in the proximal tubule and loop of Henle. Tubular secretion ensures the removal of excess salts, hydrogen ions, and other waste substances into the tubules.
The final urine, containing water, salts, and urea, passes from the collecting ducts into the renal pelvis, then through the ureter to the bladder for storage, and finally through the urethra for excretion.

The skeleton is the framework of bones and cartilage that supports and protects the body. Organisms possess different types of skeletons. The hydrostatic skeleton is found in soft-bodied animals like earthworms and is supported by fluid pressure. The exoskeleton is an external skeleton made of chitin in arthropods or calcium carbonate in mollusks. The endoskeleton, found in vertebrates, is an internal framework made of bone and cartilage.
The skeleton performs important functions: it provides shape, supports soft tissues, protects delicate organs (e.g., skull protects the brain), allows movement with muscles, forms blood cells in the bone marrow, and stores minerals such as calcium and phosphorus.
The skeletal materials include bone, cartilage, and chitin. Bone is a hard, rigid structure rich in calcium salts and collagen. Cartilage is softer, flexible, and found in areas like the ear, nose, and joints. Chitin is a strong, lightweight polysaccharide that forms the exoskeleton of insects and crustaceans.

The mammalian skeleton is divided into axial and appendicular parts. The axial skeleton consists of the skull, vertebral column, ribs, and sternum. The skull protects the brain and sensory organs. 
The vertebral column in humans is made up of 33 bones grouped into five regions: cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal vertebrae.
The cervical vertebrae (7 bones) support the head and allow neck movement.
The thoracic vertebrae (12 bones) articulate with ribs, providing support and protection for the chest cavity.
The lumbar vertebrae (5 bones) are the strongest, bearing body weight and aiding movement.
The sacral (5 fused bones) and coccygeal vertebrae (4 fused bones) form the back base, supporting the pelvis and stabilizing posture.
The appendicular skeleton includes the pectoral girdle, pelvic girdle, forelimbs, and hindlimbs. The pectoral girdle consists of scapula and clavicle, attaching forelimbs to the body. The pelvic girdle supports the hindlimbs and encloses reproductive organs. The limbs are made of long bones such as humerus, radius, ulna, femur, tibia, and fibula.
Together, the axial and appendicular skeletons provide shape, protection, and enable complex movement in mammals. Each bone is specialized for its function, such as flat bones for protection, long bones for leverage, and irregular bones for varied roles.

oints are points where two or more bones meet. They can be classified as immovable joints (e.g., sutures in the skull), slightly movable joints (e.g., between vertebrae), and freely movable joints or synovial joints. Synovial joints include hinge joints (elbow, knee), ball and socket (hip, shoulder), pivot (neck), and gliding joints (wrist, ankle). Synovial joints contain synovial fluid, ligaments, and cartilage that reduce friction and allow smooth movement.
Muscles are tissues that contract to bring about movement. There are three types: skeletal (voluntary, striated), smooth (involuntary, unstriated), and cardiac (involuntary, striated). Skeletal muscles work in pairs as antagonistic muscles, e.g., biceps and triceps, to produce movement by pulling on bones.
Muscles and joints work together with the skeleton to achieve locomotion, posture, and stability in mammals.

The alimentary canal is a long muscular tube in humans through which food passes, gets digested, absorbed, and the waste removed. It is about 9 meters long and begins at the mouth and ends at the anus.
It consists of the following parts: mouth, pharynx, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, rectum, and anus. Associated glands include salivary glands, liver, and pancreas.
The mouth receives food, where mechanical digestion by teeth and chemical digestion by saliva begin. The oesophagus transports food to the stomach by peristalsis.
The stomach is a muscular sac that churns food and secretes gastric juice containing hydrochloric acid and enzymes. The small intestine (duodenum, jejunum, ileum) is the major site of digestion and absorption of digested nutrients.
The large intestine absorbs water and forms faeces, while the rectum stores undigested waste for removal. Accessory organs like the liver produce bile for fat digestion, and the pancreas produces enzymes for digestion.

Digestive enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up the breakdown of large food molecules into smaller, soluble ones. They are secreted by glands in different regions of the alimentary canal.
In the mouth, salivary amylase breaks down starch into maltose. In the stomach, pepsin (a protease) digests proteins into peptides, while rennin (in infants) coagulates milk. Hydrochloric acid provides the acidic medium needed.
In the small intestine, pancreatic amylase continues starch digestion, trypsin breaks down proteins, and lipase digests fats into fatty acids and glycerol. Bile salts from the liver emulsify fats, aiding lipase action.
In the ileum, intestinal enzymes such as maltase, lactase, sucrase, and peptidases complete digestion, breaking disaccharides into monosaccharides and peptides into amino acids.
The final products of digestion are glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, and glycerol, which are absorbed into the blood and lymph for use by the body.

Feeding in organisms differs according to their level of complexity. Amoeba, a unicellular organism, feeds by phagocytosis. It engulfs food particles with pseudopodia, forming a food vacuole where enzymes digest the food, and the soluble nutrients diffuse into the cytoplasm.
Hydra, a simple multicellular organism, captures prey with its tentacles armed with stinging cells (cnidoblasts). The prey is paralyzed and pushed into the gastrovascular cavity, where enzymes break it down. Digested nutrients are absorbed by diffusion into body cells, while undigested food is expelled through the same opening (incomplete digestive system).
Man has a complex and complete digestive system with mouth and anus. Feeding is holozoic, involving ingestion, digestion (mechanical and chemical), absorption, assimilation, and egestion. Specialized organs and glands ensure efficient digestion and absorption of nutrients.
This shows a progressive complexity of feeding from unicellular to multicellular simple organisms and finally to higher animals like humans.

 The transport system in animals ensures the movement of materials such as nutrients, gases, hormones, and waste products. In humans, blood is the main transport medium.
 Blood is composed of plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. Plasma is a straw-colored liquid that transports nutrients, hormones, waste products, and heat.
 Red blood cells contain hemoglobin, which binds oxygen for transport to tissues and removes carbon dioxide. White blood cells provide defense against infections through phagocytosis and antibody production.
 Platelets aid in blood clotting, preventing excessive loss of blood during injury. Blood also maintains pH balance, body temperature, and water content.
 The transport system ensures that all body cells receive oxygen and nutrients needed for metabolism while wastes are removed for excretion.

The mammalian circulatory system is a double and closed system. Blood flows through the heart twice in one complete circulation.
 It consists of systemic circulation (between the heart and the body) and pulmonary circulation (between the heart and the lungs).
 The system includes the heart, blood vessels, and blood. Arteries carry blood away from the heart, veins return blood to the heart, and capillaries connect arteries to veins for exchange of substances.
 This double circulation ensures efficient oxygenation of blood and rapid supply of nutrients to tissues.
 The circulatory system also plays roles in immunity, transport of hormones, and maintenance of constant body temperature.

The heart is a muscular organ that pumps blood throughout the body. It lies in the thoracic cavity between the lungs.
 It consists of four chambers: two atria and two ventricles. The right side of the heart pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs, while the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body.
 Valves prevent backflow of blood and ensure unidirectional flow. The heartbeat is controlled by the sinoatrial node (pacemaker), which initiates impulses causing contraction.
 The heart functions through systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation).
 Blood supply to the heart muscle is provided by coronary arteries. Diseases such as coronary heart disease can impair its function.
 The heart works continuously to maintain life by ensuring constant supply of oxygen and nutrients to tissues.

Respiration is the biological process by which living organisms release energy from food substances, usually glucose. There are two main types: aerobic respiration, which requires oxygen and releases large amounts of energy, and anaerobic respiration, which occurs without oxygen and releases less energy.
In humans, the respiratory system consists of the nostrils, nasal cavity, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and alveoli. The alveoli are the main sites of gas exchange where oxygen diffuses into the blood and carbon dioxide diffuses out.
Other organisms use different respiratory organs: 
Summary of Respiratory Systems
Unicellular organisms – Cell membrane (diffusion).
Earthworm – Moist skin.
Fish – Gills.
Insects – Tracheal system (spiracles, tracheae, tracheoles).
Arachnids – Book lungs and/or tracheae.
Amphibians (Frog) – Lungs, moist skin, buccal cavity; tadpoles use gills.
Reptiles – Lungs only (well developed).
Birds – Lungs with air sacs (one-way airflow).
Mammals – Lungs with alveoli.
Flowering plants – Stomata (leaves), lenticels (stems), root hairs (roots).

Respiration is vital for energy supply in processes like growth, reproduction, movement, and repair. Lack of efficient respiration leads to fatigue and eventual death.

Breathing is the mechanical aspect of respiration, involving inhalation and exhalation. Inhalation occurs when the diaphragm contracts and flattens, and the intercostal muscles raise the rib cage, increasing the chest cavity volume and drawing in air.
During exhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and becomes dome-shaped, the rib cage moves downward, and the chest cavity volume decreases, forcing air out. This cycle is controlled by the medulla oblongata in response to carbon dioxide levels in the blood.
Gaseous exchange takes place by diffusion at the alveoli. Oxygen passes into the bloodstream and binds with hemoglobin, while carbon dioxide moves from the blood into the alveoli.
The rate of breathing can be influenced by exercise, emotions, or environmental conditions. Efficient breathing ensures adequate oxygen supply and removal of waste gases.

An aquatic habitat is a water-based environment where organisms live and interact. It can be marine, freshwater, or estuarine. Aquatic habitats are characterized by high humidity, dissolved oxygen, salinity, and water temperature.
Organisms in aquatic habitats include plants (algae, water lilies), animals (fish, crabs, amphibians), and microorganisms. Adaptations to aquatic life include gills for breathing, streamlined bodies for swimming, and air bladders for buoyancy.
Light penetration decreases with depth, affecting photosynthesis. Nutrient availability also determines biodiversity in water bodies. Human activities like pollution, overfishing, and climate change threaten aquatic habitats.

Estuarine habitats are areas where freshwater from rivers meets and mixes with saltwater from the sea. They are characterized by variable salinity, tidal influence, high nutrient content, and muddy soils.
Organisms found in estuaries include mangroves, crabs, oysters, mudskippers, and various migratory birds. Plants like mangroves have specialized roots (pneumatophores) for respiration and salt-tolerant adaptations.
Estuaries are highly productive ecosystems that serve as breeding grounds for many marine species. They help filter pollutants and act as buffers against coastal erosion.
However, they are threatened by reclamation, pollution, and overexploitation. Conservation is essential to maintain their ecological importance.

Freshwater habitats include rivers, lakes, streams, and ponds. They are characterized by low salinity, flowing or still water, variable temperature, and dissolved oxygen.

A terrestrial habitat is a land-based environment where organisms live. Examples include deserts, savannas, forests, and mountains. These habitats are influenced by factors like rainfall, temperature, light, soil type, and topography.

A forest habitat is a land area dominated by trees and vegetation with high biodiversity. Forests are classified into tropical rainforests, temperate forests, and montane forests. Tropical rainforests are dense, evergreen, and found in areas of high rainfall.
Abiotic factors in forests include light penetration, humidity, soil fertility, and temperature. Biotic factors involve producers (trees, shrubs), consumers (insects, birds, mammals), and decomposers (fungi, bacteria).
Adaptations include tall trees with buttress roots for support, climbing plants (lianas), and shade-loving undergrowth. Animals show adaptations like arboreal lifestyle, camouflage, and sharp vision. Forests regulate climate, provide oxygen, and support water cycles but face deforestation and logging threats.

        Savanna or grassland habitat is dominated by grasses with scattered trees. It is common in tropical regions with alternating wet and dry seasons, such as Nigeria.
Abiotic factors include rainfall (seasonal), temperature, soil type, and fire occurrence. Biotic factors involve grasses, shrubs, herbivores (antelopes, elephants), carnivores (lions, cheetahs), and decomposers.
Plant adaptations include deep roots to access underground water, thick bark to resist fire, and drought-resistant leaves. Animal adaptations include migration during dry seasons, grazing, speed for escaping predators, and burrowing.
Savannas are important for grazing livestock, biodiversity, and tourism but face threats such as overgrazing, bush burning, and poaching.

Population ecology is the study of groups of organisms of the same species living in a particular area at a given time. Key terms include population size, density, distribution, and growth.
Factors affecting population include natality (birth rate), mortality (death rate), immigration, and emigration. Carrying capacity refers to the maximum number of individuals the environment can support.
Population growth patterns include exponential growth (rapid increase) and logistic growth (regulated by limiting factors like food, space, and disease). Human activities, such as deforestation and pollution, also influence population changes.
Population studies are important in agriculture, wildlife management, and human resource planning.

Food shortage is the inadequate supply of food to meet the nutritional needs of a population. It can result in malnutrition, starvation, and death.
Causes include overpopulation, poor agricultural practices, climate change, desertification, pests, post-harvest losses, wars, and economic problems. Natural disasters such as droughts and floods also contribute to food shortage.
Consequences of food shortage include reduced productivity, increased poverty, diseases (kwashiorkor, marasmus), and social unrest.
Solutions include mechanized farming, irrigation, improved storage facilities, pest control, government policies, food importation, and family planning to regulate population growth. International organizations also provide aid to famine-affected areas.
